An architect document approval system is the difference between “I think they
approved it” and a project record that shows what was issued, who saw it, what was
approved and what changed after approval. For architecture firms, this matters
because drawings and documents are not passive files. They carry decisions.

AORMS keeps document approvals inside the project record. Drawings, transmittals,
client approvals, consultant comments and revision history can be reviewed without
searching through message threads.

## What needs approval

In a design office, approval can apply to many records:

- Drawings and revisions
- Design options
- Client decisions
- Consultant submissions
- Tender documents
- Site instructions
- Fee and scope confirmations

When each approval travels through a different channel, the project record becomes
fragile. AORMS gives the office a single place to preserve evidence.

## Why approval evidence matters

Approval evidence protects the practice. It shows the timeline of issue, response,
acceptance and change. If a client later questions why a drawing moved forward,
the office can point to the record instead of reconstructing events.

This also helps internal management. Principals can see which approvals are
blocking work, which clients are slow to respond and which project phases are
waiting on external movement.

## Connected to revision tracking

Approval and revision workflows are inseparable. A client may approve one option
and request a change the next week. A consultant may comment on a drawing that has
already moved to a newer revision. AORMS keeps those relationships visible.

## Why approvals disappear

Approvals disappear because architecture communication is often polite, fast and
informal. A client replies "looks fine" on WhatsApp. A consultant marks up a PDF
and sends it to one person. A principal gives a verbal go-ahead in a meeting. A
drawing moves forward because everyone believes the decision was clear. Months
later, when cost or scope is questioned, the office has to prove what happened.

AORMS is designed to avoid that fragile memory. It keeps the approval close to the
document, the project and the people involved. The point is not bureaucracy. The
point is architectural discipline: if a decision changes the work, the decision
deserves a place in the record.

## What a project lead can see

A project lead should be able to answer simple questions without searching:

- Which drawing set is current?
- Which documents were issued to the client?
- Which approvals are still pending?
- Which consultant comments need action?
- Which decision created the next revision?
- Which approval is safe to rely on?

When these answers are visible, the team works with more confidence. Juniors do
not proceed from outdated instructions. Seniors do not waste time reconstructing
email trails. Principals can review risk without entering every folder.

## Approvals as part of the office system

Document approvals connect naturally to other AORMS modules. A client approval can
affect revision history. A consultant comment can create a task. A tender document
can move to a contractor scope. An approved stage can support fee recovery. This
is why approvals should not sit in a separate tool.

Architecture offices already produce order in drawings. AORMS helps that same
order exist around decisions.

## Controlled visibility for external people

Document approval also depends on who is allowed to see what. A client may need
issued drawings and decision requests. A consultant may need an RFI thread and the
current drawing set. A contractor may need tender documents and nothing beyond
that scope. AORMS keeps these views controlled so the office does not expose its
entire internal record just to move one approval forward.

This is important for small and mid-sized firms. They need professional control
without the complexity of enterprise document systems. AORMS keeps the approval
trail close to everyday project work.

## Better discipline without heavier process

The best approval system should feel like less work, not more. AORMS gives the
office enough structure to protect the decision without forcing the team into a
separate administrative ritual. The project lead can issue, record, follow up and
review from the same operating record.

That is the practical value for architects: fewer missing approvals, fewer
uncertain drawing sets, fewer client conversations rebuilt from memory, and a
clearer line between design progress and formal acceptance.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is an architect document approval system?

It is software that records drawing issues, document reviews, client approvals,
consultant comments and approval evidence against the project.

### Can AORMS track drawing approvals?

Yes. AORMS can keep drawing and approval records connected to the project timeline
so the office knows what was issued and what was accepted.

### Does this help with client disputes?

Yes. A clear approval record helps the practice show what decision was made, when
it was made and what changed later.

### Is this only for clients?

No. Approval workflows can involve internal teams, clients, consultants,
contractors and principals depending on the document and project stage.
