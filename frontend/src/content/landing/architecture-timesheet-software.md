Most architecture practices track time twice and trust neither number. There is a
rough attendance register somewhere, and a separate mental note of who is "busy" —
but nothing that says which project a Tuesday afternoon actually went into, or
whether that time was billable. **Architecture timesheet and time tracking
software** should close that gap: attendance and time recorded against the same
projects, phases and tasks the studio already runs on, not in a standalone HR app
that knows nothing about a GFC set or a fee stage.

AORMS treats time as project data, not payroll paperwork. Attendance is marked
per person per day, and the hours attach to the project and task they belong to.
That single decision — keeping time on the project record — is what makes workload
honest and performance scoring fair.

## Attendance that belongs to the project, not a punch clock

A generic time clock records that someone was present. It cannot tell you what the
day produced. In AORMS, per-person daily attendance sits inside the same system as
the [project record](/architecture-project-management-software) — phases, tasks,
drawings and decisions — so presence and productive time are never two disconnected
spreadsheets.

For a practice this matters because the questions a principal actually asks are
project questions: is the residential job absorbing more hours than its fee allows,
who is carrying the site-supervision load this fortnight, and is anyone quietly
working past what is healthy. A punch clock cannot answer any of those. Time
attributed to projects can.

## Time attributed to projects, tasks and the billable/non-billable line

Every task in AORMS carries a classification — billable, non-billable, training,
collaboration or personal — and a work type: design communication, design
development, technical production or construction support. When time is attributed
to a task, it inherits those dimensions. So the studio does not just know that
forty hours passed; it knows how many were billable client work, how many were
internal training, and which architectural category of work they belonged to.

That distinction is the one HR-generic tools miss. A design office lives or dies on
the ratio of billable to non-billable time against the fee it agreed, and on
whether technical production is quietly eating the hours meant for design. Because
the timesheet is built from real tasks, that ratio is a by-product of doing the
work, not a separate report someone has to assemble at month end.

## Workload you can actually see

The workload view turns attributed time into a picture of the whole team: who is
loaded, who has room, and where a deadline is about to collide with a person who is
already full. Because it draws on the same task and attendance records, it stays
current without anyone maintaining a separate capacity sheet.

This is where staffing decisions stop being guesswork. Before assigning the next
project, a principal can see actual load rather than the loudest complaint, and

## From timesheets to fair performance scoring (ASPRF)

Time data is also what makes performance scoring defensible. AORMS scores each
person on a rolling 30-day composite called ASPRF — Reliability 30%, Quality 25%,
Client Impact 15%, Collaboration 15%, Learning 10%, and an opt-in Wellbeing at 5%.

The reason time matters here is delivery predictability. Every task carries an
estimated-hours figure and a difficulty coefficient (1–5). When actual attributed
time is compared against the estimate — and weighted by how hard the task was —
reliability becomes a measure of "delivered what you said, when you said," not a raw
count of tickets closed. A one-line status update and a hard detail are never scored
as equal work. That anti-gaming design is the whole point; we explain it in full in
[measuring team performance fairly](/blog/measuring-team-performance-asprf).

## How time tracking works in AORMS

1. **Mark attendance.** Each person's daily attendance is recorded, giving the
   office a per-person, per-day baseline of who was working.
2. **Attribute the time.** Hours are attached to the project, phase and task they
   went into, so time carries its billable/non-billable classification and work type
   with it.
3. **Watch workload.** The team workload view rolls those records up into current
   load per person, so over-allocation is visible before it becomes overtime.
4. **Feed ASPRF fairly.** Attributed time is compared against each task's estimated
   hours and difficulty coefficient, grounding the reliability portion of the
   30-day ASPRF score in real delivery rather than task volume.

Everything stays on your own record inside the office system, alongside GST invoicing,
fees and the [wider Indian office](/architecture-office-management-india) it runs in.

## Frequently asked questions

### How is this different from a generic HR or timesheet tool?

A generic tool records presence and exports it to payroll. AORMS attributes time to
the specific project, phase and task, and inherits each task's billable/non-billable
classification and work type. So the same record that marks attendance also tells you
your billable ratio and feeds performance scoring — without a separate reconciliation.

### Does time tracking distinguish billable from non-billable work?

Yes. Every task is classified as billable, non-billable, training, collaboration or
personal, and time attributed to a task carries that classification. That lets a
practice see how much of a fortnight was genuinely billable client work against the
fee it agreed, rather than assuming presence equals billable output.

### How does the timesheet connect to performance scoring?

Through ASPRF, the rolling 30-day composite score. Attributed time is compared with
each task's estimated hours and difficulty coefficient (1–5), so the reliability
weighting reflects delivering to estimate on hard work — not closing many trivial
tasks. It is designed to be fair and hard to game.

### Is any of this tied to the Indian financial year?

Yes. AORMS is built India-first, with the April–March financial year, GST and SAC
codes and COA fee stages part of the model. Time and billable hours read against
projects and fees in the same India-native system, so the numbers line up with how an
Indian practice bills and files.

### Can I control who sees time and attendance data?

Yes, through role-based access — the same hierarchy that governs the rest of the
office. How roles and visibility work is covered in
[team access and hierarchy](/blog/team-access-and-hierarchy).
