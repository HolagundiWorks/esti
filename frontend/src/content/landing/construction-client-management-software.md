## Construction Client Management Software

Most software for architects manages the design phase and then goes quiet. But the relationship that strains a practice is the one during **construction** — when the client wants to know why a drawing changed, the contractor raises an RFI that needs a client decision, a running bill needs sign-off, and a site instruction has fee implications. **Construction client management** is about keeping the client informed and accountable through the build, with a record behind every decision.

AORMS, powered by ESTI, carries the client relationship from proposal through the Construction Administration stage in one system.

## A scoped client portal through the build

During construction the client should see progress without being able to wander through your office. AORMS gives them a **read-only client portal** scoped to their project: issued drawings, transmittals, the approvals waiting on them, invoices, and project status. They stay informed, you stop fielding "can you send me the latest drawing?" emails, and everything they saw is recorded. The portal itself is covered on the [client management](/architecture-client-management-software) page.

## Approvals and RFIs that don't stall the site

Construction stalls when a decision sits in someone's inbox. AORMS keeps approvals on record — what was sent, the drawing revision, the date and the client's response — so a delayed sign-off is visible, not invisible. Contractors get their own scoped portal for tenders, documents and site coordination (RFIs, submittals, snags, NCRs), so queries route to the firm and answers route back with a trail. You are coordinating client and contractor from one place.

## Running bills and site instructions, with the client in the loop

PMC and construction administration is where money and scope move fastest. AORMS tracks running bills through their lifecycle (including contractor verification), logs site instructions, and records progress reports — so when a site instruction carries a fee implication or a running bill needs client awareness, it is captured against the project rather than agreed verbally and forgotten. The delivery side is detailed on the [office management](/architecture-office-management-software) page.

## Changes during construction stay billable

Construction is when "small changes" multiply. Because AORMS logs every change with its [revision source and category](/client-revision-management) and ties it to the [COA fee proposal](/coa-compliant-billing-software), a client-driven change during the build is visible against the agreed scope — so it can be billed, not absorbed. The [revision tracking](/architecture-revision-tracking) page covers the mechanics.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is construction client management software?

It is software that keeps the client informed and accountable during the construction phase — sharing drawings, transmittals, approvals, invoices and progress through a scoped portal, and recording every decision — rather than only managing the design stage. AORMS carries the relationship from proposal through Construction Administration.

### How do clients see progress during construction?

Through a read-only client portal scoped to their project: issued drawings and transmittals, pending approvals, invoices and status. They stay informed without access to the rest of the office, and everything shared is recorded.

### How are contractor RFIs and the client connected?

Contractors get their own scoped portal for site coordination — RFIs, submittals, snags and NCRs route to the firm with a trail, and decisions that need client input are recorded as approvals, so nothing stalls invisibly in an inbox.

### Can it handle running bills and site instructions?

Yes. AORMS tracks running bills through their lifecycle including contractor verification, logs site instructions and records progress reports, all against the project.

### Do construction-phase changes stay billable?

They do. Changes are logged with their revision source and category and tied to the COA fee proposal, so a client-driven change during construction is visible against the agreed scope and can be raised as additional fee.
