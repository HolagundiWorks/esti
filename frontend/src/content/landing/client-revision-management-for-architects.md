Client revision management for architects is the discipline of recording what a
client changed, when they changed it, who approved it and what it does to scope,
fees and programme. Most architecture firms already manage revisions, but the
record is scattered across email, WhatsApp, drawing folders and memory. That is
where disputes begin.

AORMS keeps revision movement inside the project record. A client request can be
classified, reviewed, approved and linked to the drawings, fee effect and task
work it creates. The practice no longer has to argue from memory.

## Why client revisions become expensive

Small changes rarely look dangerous at the moment they arrive. A wall moves, a
room changes use, a facade option is reopened, or the client asks for one more
planning option. The work feels manageable, so the team absorbs it. After ten such
changes, the office has lost billable time and the client still thinks the project
is on the original scope.

The problem is not the change itself. The problem is unrecorded change. AORMS
turns every client revision into a traceable object with impact, status and owner.

## What AORMS records

- The client request and date
- The project, phase and affected documents
- Whether the change is minor, major or critical
- The responsible owner inside the practice
- Approval status and client acknowledgement
- Follow-up tasks and fee implications

This gives principals a clean view of which clients are creating scope pressure
and which projects need commercial intervention.

## Connected to approvals and documents

Revision management only works when it is connected to document approvals. AORMS
links revision requests to drawing issues, approval workflows and project evidence.
If the client approves a change, the approval sits in the same record as the
drawing and the task created from it.

## From revision to office signal

The cognitive dashboard can surface repeated revision pressure as an office signal.
That helps the owner see risk before it turns into unpaid rework. The goal is not
to block clients; it is to make the cost of movement visible early enough to act.

## A calm way to speak about change

Architects often hesitate to formalise revisions because they do not want every
conversation to feel contractual. But a clear record does not have to make the
relationship colder. It can make the relationship calmer. When the client sees
that a request has been captured, reviewed and connected to its impact, the office
is no longer relying on tone, memory or persuasion.

AORMS gives the practice a neutral language: this changed, this is why, this is
who approved it, and this is what it affects. That language protects both sides.
The client sees the seriousness of repeated movement. The architect can continue
designing without silently absorbing every change as if it were part of the
original brief.

## What principals can review

For a principal architect, the useful question is not only "how many revisions
happened?" The better questions are:

- Which clients are creating repeated design movement?
- Which changes came from our own correction?
- Which technical queries are blocking progress?
- Which scope shifts should become fee conversations?
- Which approvals are missing before the team proceeds?

When these questions can be answered from the office record, project reviews become
more precise. A partner can intervene early, before the team spends another week
redrawing work that should have been commercially acknowledged.

## How it connects to everyday work

Revision management in AORMS is not a separate archive. It connects to tasks,
drawings, approval records, fee follow-up and the dashboard. A major client change
can create work for the project lead, a note for the principal, and a commercial
review for the fee record. A technical query can remain visible until it is
answered. An internal correction can be distinguished from client-driven movement,
which is essential for honest practice management.

This is the operating difference: revisions stop being a pile of file names and
become structured office memory.

## What changes in weekly reviews

In a weekly review, revision memory changes the tone of the meeting. Instead of
asking everyone what they remember, the principal can ask what the record shows.
Which revision came from the client? Which one was an internal correction? Which
technical query remains open? Which change has no approval? Which change should be
discussed before more drawing time is spent?

That makes revision control a management habit, not an archive exercise. The team
does not need to become defensive. They can simply work from a clearer account of
what happened.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is client revision management for architects?

It is the process of recording, classifying and approving client-driven changes so
scope, drawings, fee impact and project tasks remain traceable.

### Can AORMS classify revisions by severity?

Yes. Revision requests can be treated as minor, major or critical so the practice
knows which changes are routine and which require owner or commercial attention.

### Does this replace email?

No. Email can still be used for conversation, but AORMS becomes the authoritative
project record for the decision, approval and follow-up action.

### How does this protect fees?

When changes are recorded against the project and phase, principals can see where
client movement creates extra work and decide whether to absorb, defer or bill it.
