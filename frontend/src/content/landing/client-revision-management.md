## Client Revision Management for Architects

Ask any architect where the **revision frustration** comes from and the answer is the same: the client who keeps changing their mind, the "small tweak" that is the fourth small tweak this week, and the moment months later when they insist they never asked for any of it. Unmanaged client revisions do not just waste design hours — they erode the fee, the timeline and the relationship, and there is rarely a record to point to. **Client revision management** is about turning that mess into a record you can stand behind.

AORMS, powered by ESTI, treats every revision as data, not noise. And more than that: it enforces a sequence that prevents a client instruction from reaching the site until the architect has assessed it and the client has formally approved that assessment.

## The sequence that protects the practice

Every client revision follows a deliberate chain:

1. **Minutes of meeting** are recorded and issued to the client portal — one shared, dated record both sides read.
2. **ESTI extracts** each change discussed in those minutes as a draft revision request, with a suggested impact category.
3. **The client reviews, edits and submits** only the requests that are real — nothing reaches the office until the client actively sends it.
4. **The architect marks criticality and implications** — overriding ESTI's suggestion where judgement is needed — and records the cost, timeline and scope consequences.
5. **The client approves the architect's assessment** through the portal, creating a timestamped record of "yes, I accept this is a Major change with these implications."
6. **Only then do site or drawing changes proceed.**

This sequence is the difference between revisions that disappear into the team's workload and revisions that carry an approval, a consequence and a clear record.

## Every change carries its source and category

When a decision changes in AORMS, it is logged with two things that matter:

- **Revision source** — was this *client-driven*, an *internal error*, a *technical query*, or a *scope change*? This is the field that ends the "we never asked for that" argument.
- **Revision category** — *minor*, *major*, or *critical*, so the weight of a change is recorded, not just its existence.

Those two fields feed a **Revision Intelligence** view that shows, at a glance, how much of your rework is genuinely client-driven versus your own errors or technical queries. For the first time you can say — with evidence — "62% of this project's revisions came from the client after sign-off." ## The approval trail that protects you

A revision you cannot prove is a revision you will eat. AORMS keeps an **immutable approval and activity log**: what was issued, when, on which drawing, and who signed off. Drawing revisions are tracked against the register, and approvals sent to the client are recorded with their response. When the dispute starts, you open the record instead of searching your inbox. ## Making scope creep billable

The real cost of poor client revision management is the unbilled change. If a client-driven revision after a phase sign-off is a scope change, it is a billable event — but only if it was captured as one. Because AORMS ties revisions to decisions, projects and the [COA fee proposal](/coa-compliant-billing-software), a major client-driven change is visible against the agreed scope, so you can raise it as additional fee instead of absorbing it silently. ## From frustration to a calm record

The point is not to fight clients — it is to remove the ambiguity that causes the friction. When every change has a source, a category, an approval and a link to the fee, the conversation shifts from "did you ask for this?" to "here is the record, and here is the additional fee for the scope change you approved." That is the difference between a frustrating relationship and a professional one.

## Frequently asked questions

### How does AORMS reduce revision frustration?

It removes the ambiguity that causes friction by enforcing a deliberate sequence. Minutes go to the portal. ESTI extracts change requests. The client submits them. The architect marks criticality and implications. The client approves. Only then does the change reach the team. Every change is logged with its source (client-driven, internal error, technical query, scope change) and category (minor, major, critical), and disputes are settled by the record rather than memory.

### What is client revision management?

It is the practice of capturing every client-requested change with enough context — who asked, when, how significant, and whether it was approved — that the firm can defend its work and bill for scope creep. AORMS does this with revision source/category fields, drawing revision tracking and an immutable approval log.

### Can I prove a client approved a change?

Yes. Approvals are recorded with what was issued, the drawing revision, the date and the client's response, in an immutable log — so you can show exactly what was approved and when.

### How do I bill clients for extra revisions?

When a client-driven revision after sign-off is a scope change, AORMS shows it against the agreed COA fee proposal so you can raise it as additional fee. The [change management](/architecture-change-management-software) workflow ties the change to the billing.

### Does it show how much rework is the client's fault?

The Revision Intelligence view breaks revisions down by source, so you can see what share of rework was client-driven versus internal errors or technical queries — useful for both fee conversations and improving your own process.
