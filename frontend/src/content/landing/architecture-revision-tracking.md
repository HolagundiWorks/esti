Revisions are where architecture projects leak money. A client asks for "a small
change," the team makes it, no one records why or what it cost, and three months
later the project is over budget with no way to explain where the fee went.
**Architecture revision tracking** turns that invisible drift into a record: every
revision captured with its category, its source, and its consequence.

AORMS treats revision intelligence as a core discipline, not an afterthought —
because in most practices, unmanaged change is the single largest hidden cost.

## Why untracked revisions are so expensive

A drawing revision is never just a drawing edit. It ripples into rework hours,
re-coordination with consultants, re-issue to the contractor, and sometimes a
renegotiation of fee. When none of that is recorded, three things happen:

- The team absorbs the cost silently, and the project's margin disappears with no
  audit trail.
- The client never learns that their "small change" had a real price, so the next
  one comes just as casually.
- The practice cannot tell client-driven change apart from its own errors, so it
  cannot improve either its process or its fees.

The fix is not to resist change — change is the work. The fix is to *see* it.

## How revision tracking works in AORMS

Every revision carries two dimensions that make it legible.

### Category — how big is the change?

Each revision is classified **Minor**, **Major** or **Critical**. Minor revisions
pass through quietly. Major and Critical revisions raise an explicit flag —
accepting may affect timeline, cost or scope — before an architect or principal
signs off. The decision is made deliberately and on the record, not absorbed by
reflex.

### Source — why did it happen?

Each revision also records its source: **client-driven**, **internal error**,
**technical query**, or **scope change**. This is the dimension that changes how a
practice runs. Client-driven revisions are billable change and a negotiation point.
Internal errors are a quality signal to learn from. Scope changes are a contract
conversation. Lumping them together hides all three; separating them makes each
actionable.

## From a single drawing to the whole office

Revision data does not stay buried in one drawing. It rolls up. The office
dashboard surfaces revision risk across every live project, so a principal can see
which projects are absorbing heavy change, which clients drive the most revisions,
and where internal errors cluster. A pattern that would take a quarter to feel
becomes visible in a glance.

Because revisions sit on the same record as drawings, transmittals, fees and the
client portal, the chain stays intact: a client decision in the portal becomes a
classified revision, which links to the drawing it changed and the fee impact it
carried. Nothing falls between systems.

## Revisions, the portal and the approval chain

The revision record begins well before a drawing is changed. Meeting minutes are
issued to the client portal. ESTI extracts each change discussed as a draft
revision request. The client reviews and submits the real ones. The architect then
marks the final **criticality** (Minor, Major or Critical) and records the
**implications** — cost, timeline, scope, billability. The client receives a
notification and **approves the architect's assessment** through the portal. Only
after that approval does the change proceed to site or drawing work.

This sequence turns the revision from an informal instruction into a documented,
agreed event. The client's approval is timestamped, attached to the project record,
and feeds directly into revision intelligence — so the pattern across the project is
accurate rather than reconstructed from memory.

## Billing the change you can finally see

Once client-driven revisions are recorded distinctly, they stop being unpaid work.
A practice can point to the record — these revisions, on these dates, at this
classification — and bill or renegotiate from evidence rather than memory. The same
record that controls scope creep also recovers fee that used to vanish.

## Frequently asked questions

### How does architecture revision tracking work?

In AORMS, every revision is recorded with a category (Minor, Major or Critical) and
a source (client-driven, internal error, technical query or scope change). Major and
Critical changes require explicit sign-off, and all revisions roll up to an
office-wide revision-risk view, so change is visible, deliberate and billable.

### Why should architects track the source of a revision?

Because the source determines what to do about it. Client-driven revisions are
billable change to negotiate; internal errors are a quality signal to learn from;
scope changes are a contract conversation. Tracking only "a revision happened" hides
all three. Recording the source makes each one actionable.

### Can revision tracking reduce scope creep?

Yes. Every revision goes through a deliberate sequence before it reaches the team:
the client submits the request, the architect marks criticality and implications,
and the client approves that assessment. Reframing change as "a choice with recorded
consequences, agreed by both sides" — before any drawing work begins — controls
scope creep far more effectively than explaining the cost afterwards.

### Does revision tracking connect to billing?

Yes. Because client-driven revisions are recorded distinctly, a practice can bill or
renegotiate from the record rather than from memory — recovering fee that untracked
change used to absorb silently.

### Is drawing revision history included?

Yes. Each drawing holds its full revision history, current version, approval state
and transmittal log, and revisions link to the drawings they changed — so the
technical history and the cost history are the same record.
