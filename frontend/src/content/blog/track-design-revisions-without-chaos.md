Every architecture project lives through revisions. A design develops, a client
reacts, a consultant raises a query, a site condition forces a change. Revisions
are not a sign that something went wrong — they *are* the work. The chaos that
firms associate with revisions does not come from the revisions themselves. It
comes from tracking them badly: in drawing file names, scattered email threads, and
the memory of whoever happened to be in the room.

This article lays out a complete, practical system for tracking design revisions —
one that keeps the revisions and removes the chaos. It works whether you are a
three-person studio or a thirty-person practice, and it does not require expensive
software to begin.

## Why revision chaos happens

The chaos has a single root cause: **the revision history is not a system, it is a
folklore.** It lives in conventions that vary by person and break under pressure.

Consider the most common "revision control" in the profession: the file name.

```text
GroundFloor_Plan_Rev_C_final_FINAL_v2_clientok.dwg
```

This file name is a confession, not a record. It tells you someone tried to track
versions and lost. It does not tell you *why* revision C happened, *who* asked for
it, whether the client actually approved it, or whether it changed the fee or the
programme. When three team members each keep their own naming habits, and the
"current" file lives in someone's local folder, the truth fragments completely.

The downstream effects are predictable and expensive:

- Someone develops an **outdated revision**, and the work has to be redone.
- A contractor builds from a **superseded drawing**, and the error surfaces on site.
- At project close, "did the client approve R2 or R3?" becomes an argument no one
  can win.
- Scope creep accumulates invisibly, because the small changes were never logged.

None of this is a discipline failure by the team. It is a *systems* failure: the
firm never gave revisions a real home.

## What a real revision record contains

A revision becomes trackable — and the chaos disappears — the moment each one
captures a small, consistent set of facts. Not a bureaucratic form; just enough to
make the revision a first-class record instead of a guess.

### What changed

The revision is tied to the actual drawing or decision it affects, not floating as a
loose note. "Revised the parapet detail on A-204" is anchored to A-204, so anyone
looking at that drawing sees its history.

### Why it changed, and from whom

This is the field most firms skip, and it is the most valuable. Every revision has a
**source**:

- **Client-driven** — the client requested it.
- **Internal error** — the firm made a mistake that needed correcting.
- **Technical query** — a consultant or authority raised something.
- **Scope change** — the brief itself changed.

Recording the source turns a pile of revisions into intelligence. It tells you which
changes are potentially billable, which point to a quality issue, and which belong to
consultant coordination rather than design churn.

### How big it is

A revision also has a **category** — minor, major, or critical. A typo correction and
a re-planned floor are both "revisions," but treating them as equal-weight events
makes the record meaningless. Categorisation lets you see the *severity* of change,
not just its frequency.

### What it affects

Finally, does the revision touch the **fee**, the **timeline**, or neither? A
client-driven, major revision that affects the fee is a billing conversation you want
to have *now*, while the change is fresh — not a regret discovered at project close.

With these four facts — what, why/who, how big, what it affects — a revision stops
being a mysterious file and becomes a managed event.

## Building the workflow

Capturing those facts consistently requires a light workflow. Here is a structure
that holds up in real practice.

### Step 1: One source of truth per drawing

Decide, unambiguously, where the *current* revision of each drawing lives. Not three
copies in three folders — one canonical record, with older revisions retained as
history. This single decision eliminates the most common cause of rework: developing
or building the wrong version.

### Step 2: Log the revision at the moment it happens

The discipline that matters most is *timing*. A revision logged when it occurs — with
its source and impact — is accurate. A revision reconstructed weeks later is a guess.
Make logging a revision a thirty-second habit at the point of change, not an
end-of-project archaeology exercise.

### Step 3: Tie revisions to approvals and issues

A revision often exists to be reviewed. Connect it to the approval that follows
(approved, revise, rejected — on the record) and to the transmittal that issues it.
Now the chain — change, approval, issue — is intact, and "who approved this revision
and when was it sent" has a clean answer.

### Step 4: Review the pattern, not just the events

Once revisions carry their source, step back periodically and read the *pattern*. A
project where forty percent of revisions are client-driven is a fee conversation. A
phase generating repeated internal-error revisions is a quality signal. The workflow's
real payoff is not the individual record — it is the trend it reveals.

## Common pitfalls to avoid

- **Over-engineering the form.** If logging a revision takes five minutes, no one will
  do it. Keep it to the four facts and make it fast.
- **Tracking the *what* but not the *why*.** A revision history without sources is a
  changelog. A revision history *with* sources is intelligence. Do not skip the source.
- **Letting file names be the system.** Conventions are not control. The record must
  live somewhere durable and shared, not in naming habits.
- **Logging late.** Reconstructed revision histories are fiction. Capture at the moment.

## A worked example: one revision, end to end

Abstract principles are easy to nod along to and hard to apply, so here is a concrete
revision moving through the system.

The client, reviewing the schematic set for a residence, asks during a site visit to
swap the ground-floor flooring from tile to stone. In a chaotic practice, this becomes a
mental note, an afternoon of redrawing, and — months later — a fee no one can recover.
In a tracked practice, it becomes a clean record:

1. **At the moment of the request**, the change is logged against drawing A-101 (ground
   floor plan): source *client-driven*, category *major*, affects the *fee* (stone
   detailing and the revised schedule of finishes are extra scope).
2. **The revision is produced** against the single current version of A-101, so no one
   develops a superseded copy.
3. **The drawing is re-issued** for approval with a recipient and date; its age is now
   visible while it waits.
4. **The client approves R2**, and the decision is recorded on the drawing — date and
   remark — ending any future "did they approve this?" doubt.
5. **The approved drawing is released** to site as a transmittal, so the contractor
   builds stone, not tile.
6. **At month end**, the client-driven, fee-affecting change is already flagged, so it
   is billed as a variation while it is fresh — not absorbed by default.

Six light steps, each a few seconds of capture, and a change that would have been chaos
is instead a clean, billable, defensible record. Multiply that across a project's
hundred small changes and the difference between a tracked and an untracked practice is
the difference between control and folklore.

## How AORMS tracks revisions

AORMS treats revision intelligence as a core part of the system rather than an
afterthought. Every revision is logged against its project with a **category**
(minor / major / critical) and a **source** (client-driven, internal error, technical
query, scope change), connected to its drawing, its approval, and its transmittal.
Billable scope is flagged at the moment it happens, and the whole thing rolls up into a
**revision-health view** that shows the pattern — not just the events.

The result is exactly what this article is after: every revision retained, every
*why* recorded, and zero file-name archaeology. The revisions stay; the chaos goes.
