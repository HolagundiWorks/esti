A drawing that is finished but stuck waiting for sign-off is a stalled project. This
is one of the least appreciated truths in architecture delivery: the biggest
controllable delay is rarely the time spent *producing* drawings — it is the time
spent *approving* them. A clean, well-designed approval workflow can shorten a project
more reliably than any improvement in drafting speed, because it attacks the part of
the timeline that is most often wasted.

This guide walks through how to build an efficient drawing approval workflow for an
architecture project — the states an approval should move through, the roles involved,
the records that keep it honest, and the metrics that keep it fast. It is written to be
practical: you can start applying most of it tomorrow.

## Why approval time is the hidden delay

When a project slips, the post-mortem usually focuses on production: the team needed
more time, the design was complex. But look closely at most delayed projects and you
will find days — sometimes weeks — lost not to drawing, but to waiting. A drawing is
issued for the client's review, and then it sits. Nobody is producing anything against
it, because it is "with the client." Nobody is chasing it, because chasing it is no
one's explicit job. The programme bleeds time in silence.

The reason this delay is so common is that, in most firms, a pending approval has **no
visible age and no owner**. It is not a tracked item ticking upward on someone's
screen; it is a vague collective sense that "we're waiting." And things that are
nobody's job, with no visible cost, drift indefinitely.

An efficient approval workflow exists to make approval *visible* and *owned*, so that
waiting becomes the exception, not the default.

## The states of a drawing approval

A clean approval workflow moves each drawing through a small set of explicit states.
The states themselves are simple; the discipline is in making each one visible.

### 1. Issued

The drawing is sent for review. Critically, "issued" is a recorded event: it has a
**recipient** (who is reviewing it) and a **date** (when it went out). Without these
two facts, an approval cannot have an age, and an approval without an age is one that
will be forgotten.

### 2. Under review

The drawing is now with the reviewer — the firm, a consultant, or the client. The key
property of this state is its **visible age**. An approval that has been under review
for three days is healthy; one that has been under review for fourteen needs a nudge.
If your workflow does nothing else, make the age of pending approvals visible.

### 3. Approved or revise

The reviewer makes a decision, and the decision is **recorded** — approved, revise
(with comments), or rejected — with a date and a remark. This single record ends the
most common late-project argument: "did the client approve revision two or revision
three?" The answer should be a lookup, not a debate.

### 4. Released

An approved drawing is issued for construction, and that issue is logged as a
**transmittal** — a recorded handover with a recipient, a date, and a list of what was
sent. The transmittal is the link that prevents the most expensive site error: a
contractor building from a superseded drawing.

## The roles that make it work

States alone do not move an approval; people do. An efficient workflow assigns clear
responsibility:

- **The issuer** — the person who sends the drawing and owns the relationship with the
  reviewer.
- **The chaser** — explicitly, someone whose job it is to follow up when an approval
  ages past a threshold. This role is the one most firms leave undefined, which is
  precisely why approvals stall.
- **The recorder** — whoever logs the decision when it comes back. In a good system,
  this is built into the workflow rather than left to memory.

The single most important organisational change most firms can make is to give
approval-chasing an explicit owner. An approval that is everyone's responsibility is no
one's.

## Building the workflow, step by step

1. **Make every issue a recorded event.** When a drawing goes out for review, capture
   the recipient and the date. No exceptions — this is the foundation everything else
   rests on.
2. **Surface the age of pending approvals.** Maintain a view of what is under review and
   for how long, and review it regularly. An approval that crosses your threshold (say,
   seven days) should visibly demand a follow-up.
3. **Record decisions on the drawing.** When an approval comes back, log it against the
   drawing — approved, revise, or rejected, with the date and any comments. Never let a
   decision live only in an email or a memory.
4. **Issue releases as transmittals.** Every time an approved drawing goes to site,
   record it as a transmittal with a trail. This closes the loop and protects against
   superseded-drawing errors.
5. **Give the client a window.** Rather than emailing PDFs and waiting for replies, let
   the client see and act on pending approvals in one place. This both speeds approval
   and concentrates the conversation.

## The metrics that keep it fast

What gets measured gets managed. Two simple metrics will tell you whether your approval
workflow is healthy:

- **Average approval age** — how long, on average, drawings sit under review. Watch the
  trend; a rising number is an early warning.
- **Count of stale approvals** — how many approvals are currently past your threshold.
  This is the number to drive toward zero each week.

Neither requires sophisticated tooling to start. A weekly look at "what is waiting, and
for how long" is enough to transform approval discipline.

## Common failure points to avoid

- **No owner for chasing.** The single biggest cause of approval delay. Assign it
  explicitly.
- **No record of the decision.** If approvals live in email, "did they approve R2?"
  becomes a recurring argument. Record on the drawing.
- **No transmittal trail.** When site builds the wrong revision, the missing link is
  almost always an untracked issue. Log every release.
- **Treating the client as a black box.** A client who cannot see what is pending will
  not act on it. Give them a window.

## Three kinds of approval, three rhythms

Not every approval is the same, and treating them identically is a common source of
friction. An efficient workflow recognises three distinct kinds, each with its own
rhythm and owner.

**Internal review** is the firm checking its own work before anything leaves the office —
a senior architect signing off a junior's set, a coordination check across disciplines.
These should be fast and frequent, with a low threshold for "stale," because the work is
in-house and the cost of a held internal review is entirely your own. The owner is always
someone in the firm.

**Client approval** is the one most associated with delay, because the firm does not
control the reviewer. Here the workflow's job is to make the approval *easy for the client
to give* — a clear, single place to see what is pending and act — and to make its age
visible so the firm follows up before the programme suffers. The owner is the firm member
managing that client relationship; the bottleneck is usually the client's attention, which
a portal and a timely nudge directly address.

**Authority or statutory approval** — submissions to the sanctioning body — runs on a
different clock entirely, often weeks, and is largely outside the firm's control. The
workflow's role here is not to speed it but to *track* it accurately, so the project plan
reflects reality and dependent work is not started prematurely against an approval that has
not landed.

Conflating these three is what produces the familiar mess: a quick internal check waits as
long as a statutory submission because both are just "approvals" in an undifferentiated
list. Separating them — by kind, owner, and expected rhythm — lets each move at the speed it
should.

### Scaling the workflow across many projects

A workflow that works on one project must also work across twenty running at once. The
scaling test is simple: can you see, in one view, *every* approval across the whole office
that is past its threshold, regardless of project? If the answer is yes, the firm can direct
attention to where it is needed instead of relying on each project manager to remember their
own stuck items. If the answer is no — if approval state is trapped inside each project —
then the quiet, stalled approval on the project nobody is actively thinking about is exactly
the one that will slip. Office-wide visibility is what turns a per-project habit into a
practice-wide discipline.

## How AORMS supports an efficient approval workflow

AORMS treats drawings, approvals, and transmittals as connected, first-class records. An
issued drawing carries its recipient and date; pending approvals surface on the
dashboard with their **age**, so stale ones demand attention before they cost the
programme; decisions are recorded on the drawing; and every release is logged as a
transmittal. Issued drawings and transmittals are exposed to clients and contractors
through scoped portals, so the people who need to approve or build can do so directly.

The effect is to turn approval from the slowest, most invisible part of delivery into
one of the fastest and most visible — which, for most projects, is where the largest
schedule savings actually live.
