Ask a principal architect why a project went badly and you will rarely hear "the
design was wrong." You will hear that the client went quiet at a critical moment,
that an approval dragged on for weeks, that a decision made on a phone call was
later disputed, or that nobody could say who agreed to what. In other words: the
project was lost on **communication**, not design.

This is uncomfortable, because communication feels like the soft, unglamorous part
of practice — the part that happens between the real work. But for most firms it is
the single largest source of avoidable loss: lost time, lost fees, lost goodwill,
and sometimes lost clients. This article looks closely at *why* client
communication fails in architecture practices, what it actually costs, and the
specific, practical changes that fix it.

## Communication is the project's nervous system

A building project is a long chain of decisions made by people who are not in the
same room: the client, the principal, the project team, consultants, and
contractors. The drawings are the visible output, but the decisions that shape them
travel through communication. When that nervous system is healthy, decisions move
quickly and leave a trace. When it is unhealthy, decisions stall, get lost, or
change silently — and the drawings drift out of sync with what was actually agreed.

The mistake most firms make is treating communication as *messaging* — more emails,
faster replies, a busier WhatsApp group. But volume is not the problem. The problem
is **structure**. A practice can send a thousand messages a week and still lose
projects, because none of those messages becomes a durable, findable record of what
was decided.

## The four places client communication breaks

In practice, communication failure is not one thing. It is four distinct failure
modes, and most struggling projects suffer from several at once.

### 1. Decisions with no record

A client approves a material change on a site visit. The principal nods, makes a
mental note, and moves on. Three weeks later, the change has rippled into a dozen
drawings, the fee has quietly grown, and nobody can point to the moment it was
agreed. When the invoice arrives, the client genuinely does not remember approving
anything extra — and they are not lying. The decision was real, but it was never
written down.

Undocumented decisions are the most expensive kind of communication failure because
they are invisible until they detonate. The cost is deferred to the worst possible
moment: the end of the project, when the relationship and the final payment are both
on the line.

### 2. Approvals that age in silence

Most delays in architecture delivery are not drawing time — they are **approval
time**. A drawing is issued for the client's sign-off and then it sits. No one owns
chasing it. The team, assuming the client is reviewing, moves on to other work.
Days become weeks. By the time anyone notices, the programme has slipped and the
client is irritated that "nothing is happening" — even though the thing that was not
happening was *their* approval.

The root issue is that a pending approval has no *visible age* and no *owner*. It is
not on anyone's screen as a number ticking upward. It exists only as a vague sense
that "we're waiting on the client."

### 3. Conversation scattered across channels

The modern practice runs its client relationships across email, WhatsApp, phone
calls, site WhatsApp groups, and the occasional in-person meeting. Each channel is
reasonable on its own. Together they fragment the truth. A key instruction lives in
a WhatsApp voice note; a fee agreement is buried in an email thread; a design
preference was mentioned on a call that no one minuted.

When the conversation is scattered, three things follow. The same question gets
asked and answered repeatedly. Context lives in one person's phone, so it vanishes
when they are on leave. And reconstructing "what was agreed" at project close
becomes detective work across five apps.

### 4. Mismatched expectations

Sometimes the communication is frequent and well-recorded, and the project still
suffers — because the client and the firm were never explicitly aligned on *how*
they would work together. How many design options are included? What is the
turnaround on a review? What counts as a billable change? When these are left
implicit, every interaction carries a small friction, and friction compounds into
distrust.

## What poor communication actually costs

It is easy to treat communication problems as merely annoying. They are, in fact,
expensive, and the cost shows up in four ledgers.

- **Time.** Idle team days while an approval sits. Hours spent reconstructing
  decisions. Repeated answers to the same question.
- **Fees.** Scope creep that was never billed because the changes were never logged.
  This is, for many firms, the single largest leak in the practice.
- **Schedule.** Approval delays push milestones, which push billing, which strains
  cash flow.
- **Relationship.** A client who feels unheard, or who is surprised by a bill, is a
  client who does not refer you — and may not return.

Crucially, none of these costs announces itself. There is no alarm when an approval
has been waiting eleven days, no notification when the fifth "small" change of the
month lands. The losses accumulate quietly, which is exactly why firms underestimate
them.

## What good client communication looks like

Good communication in an architecture practice is not about being more responsive or
more charming. It is about making the *important* parts of the conversation —
decisions, approvals, and document issues — **structured and durable**. Five
principles separate firms that communicate well from those that lose projects.

### Decisions become records

Every meaningful client decision is logged against the project: what was decided,
who approved it, when, and what it affected (scope, fee, timeline). The casual chat
can stay casual; the *outcome* lands on the record. This single habit eliminates the
most expensive failure mode — the undocumented decision — at a stroke.

### Approvals have an owner and a visible age

A pending approval is not a vague feeling. It is a tracked item with a recipient, a
date issued, and an age that is visible at a glance. When an approval crosses a
threshold — say, more than a week — it surfaces, so someone follows up *before* the
client has to wonder why nothing is moving.

### The client has one window

Rather than a forwarded PDF and a "please confirm" email, the client has a single
place to see project status, issued drawings, invoices, and pending decisions. This
does two things: it makes the client feel in control, and it concentrates the
client-facing conversation into one durable surface instead of a dozen threads.

### Expectations are set explicitly

At the outset, the firm and client agree — in writing — on the rhythm of work:
review turnaround, number of included options, what constitutes a billable change.
This is not bureaucracy; it is the foundation that prevents a hundred small
frictions later.

### The office can see the whole picture

Finally, no single person should be the sole holder of a project's communication
state. The firm should be able to see, across all projects, which approvals are
stuck, which clients are waiting, and which decisions changed the fee. Communication
health becomes something the *practice* monitors, not something that depends on one
principal's memory.

## A practical path to fixing it

You do not need to overhaul everything at once. The highest-leverage changes, in
order:

1. **Start logging client decisions on the project, immediately.** Even a simple,
   consistent note — decision, approver, date, impact — captured at the moment, will
   pay for itself within a month.
2. **Make approvals trackable.** Give every issued drawing a recipient and date, and
   review pending approvals weekly so stale ones get chased.
3. **Give clients a single window.** Move status, drawings, and pending decisions
   into one place the client can see, so the conversation stops living in your inbox.
4. **Write down the working agreement.** Set review turnaround and billable-change
   rules up front, and refer to them when scope creeps.
