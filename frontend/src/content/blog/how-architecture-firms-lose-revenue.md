When a practice has a thin year, the instinct is to blame the obvious things: fees quoted
too low, a slow market, one client who did not pay. Those happen. But for most Indian
architecture practices, the larger loss is quieter and more structural. It is not money
that was stolen or badly priced — it is money that was genuinely earned and simply never
turned into an invoice.

Revenue leakage rarely arrives as a single dramatic event you could push back on. It
seeps out one reasonable decision at a time, in places that never appear on a profit
statement because the loss is an *absence* — an invoice that was never raised. This
article walks through the five most common leakage points in a design-led practice, why
each one hides so well, and the single, cheap habit that closes all of them: recording a
fact at the moment it happens, while there is still time to bill it.

## 1. Client revisions you absorb for free

The most common leak is the revision you never charged for. It almost never announces
itself. No client says "redesign the ground floor for the same fee." Instead it is a drip
of small, individually reasonable requests — one more option for the staircase, the
elevation in an alternative cladding, the kitchen moved "only slightly." Each is easy to
say yes to. Refusing any single one feels petty. So you absorb them, and by project close
you have delivered a substantially larger project than the brief specified, for the
original fee.

The cost is cumulative but the decisions are individual, so without a record you never see
the accumulating total — and the moment for a fee conversation never feels big enough to
arrive. The fix is not to refuse changes but to log each one as it happens, with its source
(client-driven, internal error, technical query, scope change) and whether it touches the
fee. Client-driven and scope changes are usually billable variations; internal errors are
cost you rightly absorb. Recorded live, the billable ones become a number you can act on
while the project is still open. We go deep on this in
[the hidden cost of untracked revisions](/blog/hidden-cost-untracked-revisions), and the
mechanics of capturing them cleanly are covered under
[architecture revision tracking](/architecture-revision-tracking).

## 2. COA fee stages you deliver but never invoice

The Council of Architecture's Conditions of Engagement break a project fee into work
stages — concept design, preliminary design, statutory-approval drawings, working drawings
and tender documents, construction supervision, and completion — each carrying a defined
share of the total fee. The intent is that you bill as you complete each stage.

In practice, delivery runs ahead of billing. You finish working drawings while invoicing is
still parked at the preliminary-design instalment — sixty percent of the value delivered,
thirty percent billed. That gap is not profit you are waiting on; it is profit you are
quietly *financing* for the client, interest-free, and it widens the longer the project
runs. Some of it never gets billed at all: a stage completed in a busy month, the invoice
left on a mental to-do list, then buried by the next project.

Two things close this leak. Structure the proposal around COA stages from the outset, so
each stage has a fee value attached before work begins — see
[fee-proposal structure](/architect-fee-proposal-software) and
[COA-compliant billing](/coa-compliant-billing-software). Then tie the invoice to stage
completion rather than to whenever someone remembers. When "working drawings complete" is a
recorded event on the project, the invoice it should trigger stops being invisible.

## 3. Scope creep that leaves no trace

Scope creep is revision leakage's bigger cousin: not a change to a drawing but a change to
the *job*. The client adds an outbuilding, asks for an interiors package that was never in
the brief, or expands the site. Because architectural work is fluid, these expansions blend
into "the project" without a hard line marking where the original scope ended and new,
billable work began.

The damage at project close is twofold. You cannot bill for scope you never demarcated, and
if you try, it becomes an argument of memory against memory — which the client, holding the
final payment, usually wins. A dated record of what was agreed, and of each change request
against it, converts an unwinnable dispute into a simple reference. This is where issued
minutes of meeting earn their keep: minutes capturing "client requested X on this date" are
the trail that lets you raise a variation without acrimony. More on the dispute side in
[why "we never approved that" happens](/blog/we-never-approved-that).

## 4. Approvals that stall, and billing that stalls with them

A quieter leak lives in the gap between issuing something for a decision and getting it
back. A drawing waiting three weeks for client sign-off, a consultant sitting on an RFI, an
instalment that cannot be raised until the client formally accepts the stage — each is dead
time during which nobody works and no invoice goes out. Because no task is active during the
wait, it never shows on a task list, yet it is often the single largest chunk of a project's
elapsed calendar, and it directly delays the cash tied to that milestone.

You cannot always make a client decide faster, but you can measure the wait and stop it from
hiding the billing. Record the date each item is issued and the date the decision returns.
Long turnarounds concentrated on one client become the evidence you need to justify an
extension of time — and an approved stage becomes a visible trigger to invoice rather than a
step that silently passes. See
[approval workflow](/architecture-approval-workflow-software) for how a documented issue-
and-return trail supports both.

## 5. Site supervision you do but never bill

Construction-stage supervision is billable work under the COA fee stages, yet it leaks more
than any other because it is delivered in small, scattered units. A site visit to resolve a
query, a site instruction issued to the contractor, a snag list, a progress report — each
is real professional work, and each is easy to do as a favour without ever attaching it to
an invoice. Over a long construction phase, a practice can give away months of supervision
one visit at a time.

The remedy is to treat every site touchpoint as a recorded deliverable: log the visit, the
instruction, the inspection, the progress report against the project. Once the supervision
work exists as a record, the fee stage it belongs to can be billed on evidence rather than a
vague sense that "we've been going to site a lot." Capturing that trail is what
[site-visit and supervision tracking](/architecture-site-visit-software) is for.

## The common thread: a record made at the moment

All five leaks share one cause. In every case the work was done and the money was earned;
what was missing was a fact recorded when it occurred — this revision was client-driven,
this stage is complete, this scope changed, this approval came back, this site visit
happened. The loss is not a failure of effort or honesty but of *visibility* — and
visibility is cheap. It costs one small act of recording, done live, instead of a
reconstruction from memory and email at project close, when the moment to bill has passed.

You do not need software to start — a disciplined spreadsheet and the habit will surface
most of it. What a practice-management system like AORMS adds is making that capture a
by-product of the work: a revision logged with its source, a COA stage marked complete, a
[GST invoice](/gst-billing-software-architects-india) raised against that stage, an approval
trail, a site visit recorded — each feeding the next, so the number is already there when
you go looking. For a broader view of the metrics that expose these leaks, see
[the architecture practice KPIs that actually matter](/blog/architecture-firm-kpis-that-matter).
The leaks are quiet, but not mysterious. Record the moment, and the money stops walking out
unnoticed.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much revenue does a typical practice lose to unbilled scope?

There is no honest single figure, and any vendor quoting one is guessing. To estimate your
own, take three or four recently completed projects and have the team reconstruct how many
client-driven changes and site touchpoints were absorbed rather than billed, then attach a
conservative hour cost to each. Most teams doing this for the first time are startled by the
total — which is the point: the number was invisible only because nobody recorded it as it
happened.

### Are client revisions actually billable under a COA-style engagement?

Changes that arise from the client altering a brief you had already progressed, or from a
genuine expansion of scope, are ordinarily billable as additional services or variations —
provided the change is recorded and, ideally, acknowledged before the extra work is done.
Revisions caused by your own error are not. The distinction is exactly why logging each
revision by *source* matters: it is what separates recoverable fee from cost you should
absorb.

### How does slow client approval turn into lost revenue rather than just delay?

Two ways. Where an invoice instalment is tied to a stage the client must formally accept,
the cash cannot be raised until the approval returns, so a slow decision directly delays
billing. And the idle wait consumes schedule that later gets blamed on the practice unless
you have a dated record showing the drawing was issued and sat waiting. Timestamping the
issue-and-return of every approval protects both the cash and the programme.

### Do I need to charge separately for every site visit?

Not necessarily line-by-line — construction supervision is usually covered by the relevant
COA fee stage. The point is that the stage should actually be billed, and billed on
evidence. Recording each visit, instruction, and progress report means the supervision fee
rests on a documented trail of work delivered, rather than being quietly given away because
no one attached it to an invoice.

### Does any of this change how GST applies to the fee I recover?

No — recovering previously unbilled fee follows the same rules as any architecture-services
invoice: 18% GST under SAC 998321, split as CGST and SGST within the state or charged as
IGST across states, on the same FY-sequential numbering. Recording the work live does not
create new tax; it simply means the invoice gets raised at all, on the value you earned.
