When a practice has a thin year, the instinct is to blame the obvious things: fees quoted
too low, a slow market, a client who did not pay. Those happen. But for most Indian
architecture practices the larger loss is quieter — not money stolen or badly priced, but
money genuinely earned that never turned into an invoice. It seeps out one reasonable decision
at a time, in places that never show on a profit statement because the loss is an *absence*.
This article walks through the five most common leakage points in a design-led practice, and
the one cheap habit that closes them all: recording a fact the moment it happens, while there
is time to bill it.

## 1. Client revisions you absorb for free

The most common leak is the revision you never charged for. No client says "redesign the
ground floor for the same fee." It arrives as a drip of small, reasonable requests — one
more staircase option, the kitchen moved "only slightly," the elevation shown in another
cladding. Each is easy to say yes to; refusing one feels petty. So you absorb them, and by
project close have delivered a substantially larger project than the brief specified, for the
original fee.

The decisions are individual but the cost cumulative, so no single request feels big enough
to trigger a fee conversation. Log each change as it happens, with its source (client-driven,
internal error, technical query, scope change) and whether it touches the fee. Client-driven
and scope changes are usually billable variations; internal errors are cost you rightly
absorb. Recorded live, the billable ones become a number you can act on while the project is
open — more in
[the hidden cost of untracked revisions](/blog/hidden-cost-untracked-revisions) and
[architecture revision tracking](/architecture-revision-tracking).

## 2. COA fee stages you deliver but never invoice

The Council of Architecture's Conditions of Engagement break a project fee into work stages —
concept design, preliminary design, statutory-approval drawings, working drawings and tender
documents, construction supervision, and completion — each carrying a defined share of the
fee, meant to be billed as you complete it.

In practice, delivery runs ahead of billing. You finish working drawings while invoicing sits
at the preliminary-design instalment: sixty percent of the value delivered, thirty percent
billed. That gap is profit you are quietly *financing* for the client, interest-free; some of
it never gets invoiced at all.

Structure the proposal around COA stages from the outset, so each carries a fee value before
work begins — see [fee-proposal structure](/architect-fee-proposal-software) and
[COA-compliant billing](/coa-compliant-billing-software). Then tie the invoice to stage
completion: when "working drawings complete" is a recorded event, the invoice it should
trigger stops being invisible.

## 3. Scope creep that leaves no trace

Scope creep is revision leakage's bigger cousin: not a change to a drawing but to the *job* —
the client adds an outbuilding, asks for an interiors package never in the brief, or expands
the site. Because architectural work is fluid, they blend into "the project" with no line
marking where the original scope ended and new, billable work began.

The damage at close is twofold. You cannot bill for scope you never demarcated, and if you
try it becomes memory against memory — which the client, holding the final payment, usually
wins. A dated record of what was agreed, and of each change request against it, turns an
unwinnable dispute into a reference; issued minutes of meeting are that trail, letting you
raise "client requested X on this date" as a variation without acrimony. More on that in
[why "we never approved that" happens](/blog/we-never-approved-that).

## 4. Approvals that stall, and billing that stalls with them

A quieter leak lives in the gap between issuing something for a decision and getting it back.
A drawing waiting three weeks for client sign-off, or an instalment that cannot be raised
until the client accepts the stage — each is dead time during which nobody works and no
invoice goes out. No task is active during the wait, so it never shows on a task list, yet it
is often the largest chunk of a project's calendar and delays the cash tied to that milestone.

You cannot always make a client decide faster, but you can measure the wait. Record the date
each item is issued and the date the decision returns. Long turnarounds on one client become
evidence for an extension of time — and an approved stage becomes a visible trigger to invoice
rather than a step that silently passes. See
[approval workflow](/architecture-approval-workflow-software) for how an issue-and-return
trail supports both.

## 5. Site supervision you do but never bill

Construction-stage supervision is billable under the COA fee stages, yet it leaks more than
any other because it is delivered in small, scattered units. A visit to resolve a query, a
site instruction to the contractor, a snag list, a progress report — each is real work, easy
to do as a favour without ever invoicing it. Over a long construction phase, a practice can
give away months of supervision one visit at a time.

The remedy is to treat every site touchpoint as a recorded deliverable — log the visit, the
instruction, the inspection, the progress report. Once the supervision exists as a record, the
fee stage can be billed on evidence rather than a vague sense that "we've been going to site a
lot." Capturing that trail is what
[site-visit and supervision tracking](/architecture-site-visit-software) is for.

## The common thread: a record made at the moment

All five leaks share one cause: a fact never recorded when it occurred — this revision was
client-driven, this stage complete, this approval returned, this site visit happened. The
loss is not a failure of effort or honesty but of *visibility* — and visibility is cheap: one
act of recording, done live, instead of a reconstruction from memory at project close, when
the moment to bill has passed.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much revenue does a typical practice lose to unbilled scope?

There is no honest single figure, and any vendor quoting one is guessing. To estimate your
own, take three or four recent projects and have the team reconstruct how many client-driven
changes and site touchpoints were absorbed rather than billed, at a conservative hour cost
each. Most teams are startled by the total — invisible only because nobody recorded it live.

### Are client revisions actually billable under a COA-style engagement?

Changes from the client altering a brief you had already progressed, or a genuine expansion
of scope, are ordinarily billable as additional services or variations — provided the change
is recorded and acknowledged before the extra work is done. Revisions caused by your own error
are not, which is why logging each revision by *source* matters.

### How does slow client approval turn into lost revenue rather than just delay?

Two ways. Where an instalment is tied to a stage the client must accept, the cash cannot be
raised until the approval returns, so a slow decision directly delays billing. And the idle
wait consumes schedule later blamed on the practice unless a dated record shows the drawing
was issued and sat waiting. Timestamping every approval protects the cash and the programme.

### Do I need to charge separately for every site visit?

Not necessarily line-by-line — construction supervision is usually covered by the relevant
COA fee stage. The point is that the stage should actually be billed, on evidence. Recording
each visit, instruction, and progress report means the fee rests on a documented trail, rather
than being given away because no one attached it to an invoice.

### Does any of this change how GST applies to the fee I recover?

No — recovering unbilled fee follows the same rules as any architecture-services invoice: 18%
GST under SAC 998321, split as CGST and SGST within the state or IGST across states, on the
same FY-sequential numbering. Recording the work live creates no new tax; it simply means the
invoice gets raised at all, on the value you earned.
