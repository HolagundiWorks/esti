Almost every serious dispute an architecture practice has with a client eventually
compresses into four words: *"We never approved that."* The cladding that was signed off
in March. The layout the client now says they "always had concerns about." The variation
the contractor built from your issued drawing, which the client is now refusing to pay for.

In the moment, you know you're right. You remember the meeting. You're *sure* there was an
email. But "sure" is not a record, and a dispute is decided by records — so you open your
inbox and start scrolling, and the longer you scroll, the weaker your position feels. The
problem was never your memory. It was that the approval lived somewhere it could be denied.

## Why approvals slip through the cracks

Approvals in most practices are implicit. A drawing goes out "for approval." The client
says "looks good" in a meeting, or replies "ok proceed" to one email in a thread of
twenty, or simply goes quiet and you take silence as consent. Then the project moves on.

There are three weaknesses hiding in that flow:

1. **The approval isn't tied to a specific version.** "Looks good" against *which* revision
   of the drawing? When the drawing changes twice more, the approval becomes meaningless —
   nobody can say what was actually agreed.
2. **It isn't tied to a person and a time.** A defensible record says *who* approved *what*
   *when*. A WhatsApp "ok" from an unknown number at an unknown date is not that.
3. **It can be re-narrated.** Months later, both sides remember the past in the shape of
   their current interest. Without a fixed record, the loudest and most confident version
   wins — and clients are often very confident.

## The fix is structural, not behavioural

You can't solve this by being more diligent about saving emails, any more than you can
solve a leaking roof by mopping faster. The fix is to make approval a *structured event*
rather than a conversational one.

A real approval record answers, immutably:

- **What** was issued — the specific drawing or document, at a specific revision.
- **Who** it went to, and what they responded.
- **When** each of those happened.

And critically, it can't be quietly edited afterward to match how someone now wishes it
had gone. The moment approval becomes a recorded event against a versioned drawing, "we
never approved that" stops being a viable claim — because you can show, in seconds, exactly
what they approved and when.

## It pairs with revision tracking

Approvals and revisions are two halves of the same record. A revision is a *change*; an
approval is *consent to a state*. Track one without the other and you still have gaps — you
can show the drawing changed but not that it was signed off, or that something was approved
but not which version. Tracked together, they form a continuous, defensible history of the
project: every change with its [source and category](/client-revision-management), every
issued drawing with its approval.

## The quiet benefit

The obvious value is winning the argument. The quieter, bigger value is *not having the
argument at all*. When clients know — because you told them at the start — that every issue
and approval is on the record, the casual "let's just change this and not make a fuss about
it" conversations slow down. People are more careful about what they ask for when they know
it's written down. A good approval record doesn't just protect you in a dispute; it
prevents the behaviour that causes disputes.
