Every architect knows this meeting. Ninety minutes with the client, four design
changes agreed across the table, everyone nods, everyone leaves — and then
**nothing arrives in writing**.

Not because the client changed their mind. Because writing a formal change
request is homework, and clients don't do homework. They send a WhatsApp voice
note three days later, or worse, they mention it again at the *next* meeting as
if it had been formally raised at the last one.

So the office absorbs the change. No record, no category, no fee conversation.
The revision happened — it just never *existed*.

## The gap is between the minutes and the request

Most practices already keep minutes of meeting. The minutes say *"Client asked
to rework the staircase as a spiral stair — structural review needed."* That
sentence is a **MAJOR revision with fee impact**, sitting in a paragraph nobody
will act on.

For that sentence to protect anyone, someone has to convert it into a formal
revision request: a subject, a clear description, an impact category, a record
the office can assess and the client can point to later. The architect
shouldn't write it — it's the client's request. The client won't write it —
they hate writing them.

That conversion is exactly the kind of work ESTI exists for.

## How it works in AORMS

1. **The architect records the minutes** — in the project workspace, under
   Minutes. Title, date, attendees, and what was actually discussed. Issuing
   the minutes locks them into the project's document register, the same way
   drawings and transmittals are issued.

2. **The client sees the same minutes** — issued minutes appear in the client
   portal. One record, both sides reading the same words. Draft minutes stay
   office-internal.

3. **ESTI reads them and drafts the revision requests** — the client presses
   one button. ESTI extracts every change discussed in the minutes and drafts
   each one as a ready-to-send request: a subject line, a polite specific
   description, and a suggested impact category — minor, major, or critical.

4. **The client reviews and sends** — every draft is editable. Nothing reaches
   the office until the client explicitly sends it. Discard what ESTI got
   wrong, adjust the wording, then send the ones that are real.

5. **The office receives a formal change request** — it lands in the same
   client-requests queue the office already runs: conversation threads, impact
   assessment (does it affect cost? timeline? is it billable?), and the
   revision statistics that feed the project's revision intelligence.

6. **The architect marks criticality and implications** — the office reviews each
   incoming request and sets the revision category: minor, major, or critical. Along
   with the category, the implications are recorded: does this change affect the fee?
   The programme? A structural element? A statutory approval already issued? This is
   the assessment that turns a client request into a managed change — and it is done
   by the architect, not delegated to ESTI.

7. **The client approves the implications** — the assessed implications are sent back
   through the portal for the client to acknowledge. The client sees: *"You have
   requested X. The practice assesses this as a major change that will affect the fee
   and the working drawings stage."* Until the client acknowledges, the revision is
   flagged as pending scope sign-off. A client who sees the implications before work
   begins rarely disputes them afterwards.

8. **Only then do site and drawings move** — once the client has approved the
   implications, the revision is cleared for production. Drawings are updated, site
   instructions are issued, and consultant coordination proceeds on the basis of a
   change the client has both requested and understood. The revision that used to
   seep into the project invisibly is now a dated, approved, categorised record with
   the client's sign-off on its scope — before a single line is redrawn.

The client never wrote a revision request. The office never chased one. The
architect assessed the implications and the client approved them. And the
change that used to vanish into a voice note is now a numbered, categorised,
dated record — with scope sign-off — traceable back to the exact meeting where
it was agreed, and cleared before a single drawing changes.

## What ESTI will not do

The same honesty that applies everywhere in AORMS applies here:

- **ESTI drafts; it never sends.** Every request needs the client's explicit
  push. There is no auto-submission, ever.
- **Only what's in the minutes.** ESTI extracts changes that were actually
  recorded — it doesn't invent scope, fees, or commitments. If the minutes
  contain no changes, it says so.
- **Every run is on the record.** Portal ESTI runs are logged like every AI
  Studio run — who asked, which minutes, what came back.
- **Your AI, your rules.** It runs through the same engine the office already
  controls: on-server Ollama by default, or your own OpenAI-compatible API key in Company settings,
  gated by the firm's AI settings.

## Why this matters for fees

AORMS classifies every revision by source — client-driven, internal error,
technical query, scope change — because only one of those should ever be free.
That classification is only as good as the records feeding it.

When revision requests come through the portal instead of through memory, the
project's revision history stops being an argument and becomes arithmetic:
*six client-driven changes this stage, two major, here are the dated records,
here is the fee conversation.* The client saw every one of those records before
sending it. That's what makes the conversation calm.

The office stops absorbing invisible work. The client stops doing homework.
The minutes finally do their job.
