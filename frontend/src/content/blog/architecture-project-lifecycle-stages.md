Every architecture project, from a single-plot residence in Mysuru to a campus in
Pune, moves through the same broad arc: someone enquires, a design takes shape, the
statutory bodies sign off, the drawings become buildable, the building goes up, and the
keys change hands. The names differ from office to office, but the sequence is stable —
and the Council of Architecture (COA) has already codified it. Treating that arc as a set
of *stages*, each with its own outputs and decisions, is the difference between a practice
that runs its projects and one that is run by them.

This is a plain walk-through of that lifecycle for Indian practice, with an eye on the one
question that trips up most young offices: what does each stage actually *produce* — which
documents, which client decisions, which invoice — before you are allowed to move on.

## Why think in stages at all

The COA's *Conditions of Engagement and Scale of Charges* does not describe architecture
as one continuous effort. It breaks Comprehensive Architectural Services into defined
stages and ties the release of your fee to completing each one. That structure is
practical, not bureaucratic: a project is a long chain of decisions, and each stage is a
gate. You do not develop working drawings until the preliminary design is approved; you do
not call tenders until the drawings are frozen. Skip a gate — start production on an
unapproved concept, order steel against a superseded drawing — and the cost of the mistake
compounds with every stage it survives. The stages are how you keep the client's approval,
your fee, and your drawings pointing at the same version of reality.

## The lifecycle, stage by stage

### 1. Enquiry and brief

Before COA Stage 1 even begins, there is an enquiry and a conversation. The output here is
not a drawing — it is *understanding*. You capture the site (plot, survey number,
orientation, local authority), the client's programme and budget, and the applicable
regulations: FAR, setbacks, ground coverage, height limits, and whether RERA registration
will apply.

The decisions this stage produces are the ones everything else rests on: the scope of
services, the fee, and the terms. This is where a fee proposal and a scope agreement are
signed. Get the scope wrong here and every later revision argument traces back to it.

### 2. Concept design (COA Stage 1)

Now the design begins. Concept design translates the brief into spatial ideas — bubble
diagrams, massing options, a schematic plan, and a rough order-of-cost estimate. The
deliverables are deliberately loose: enough to test the direction, not so detailed that
reworking them is expensive.

The decision this stage produces is the most important one in the whole project: *the
client chooses a direction.* Record that choice. A concept sign-off — dated, against a
specific set of sketches — is what protects you when, three months later, someone asks why
the plan looks nothing like the "first idea" they half-remember. This is the first client
approval worth putting in writing.

### 3. Preliminary design and statutory approvals (COA Stages 2–3)

Preliminary design develops the chosen concept into resolved plans, sections, and
elevations, with materials and a firmer cost estimate. In parallel — and this is very
much an Indian-practice reality — you prepare the drawings the statutory authorities need:
the sanction/building-permit set for the local body (BBMP, BDA, the municipal corporation,
or the development authority in your city), plus any fire, environmental, or airport-height
clearances the project attracts.

This stage produces two streams of documents: the developed design set the client approves,
and the submission set the authority approves. Both are gates. The building permit /
commencement certificate is a hard dependency — construction that starts without it is
unauthorised. Track the submission the way you track a drawing revision, because it *is*
one: submitted, queried, resubmitted, sanctioned.

### 4. Working drawings and tender (COA Stages 4–5)

This is the heaviest production stage. Working drawings (GFC — "good for construction")
translate the approved design into buildable detail: dimensioned plans, sections, details,
schedules for doors, windows and finishes, and coordination with structural, MEP and other
consultants. Alongside them you prepare the tender documents — specifications, the schedule
of quantities / BOQ, and the conditions of contract — so the work can be priced and a
contractor appointed.

Two things define this stage. First, **version control becomes non-negotiable**: with
dozens of sheets and multiple consultants, the single most expensive mistake is building
from a superseded drawing. A disciplined [drawing revision and approval
workflow](/drawing-revision-tracking-software) — current versus superseded, who approved
what, when — is what keeps the site building the right thing. Second, **every change now
has a source**: a client change, a consultant's technical query, a code correction, or a
genuine scope change. Recording *why* each revision happened is what later tells you which
changes you can bill for and which you absorbed.

### 5. Construction and site supervision (COA Stage 6)

The role shifts from author to supervisor. As the consultant architect you are not the
builder — you visit, inspect, instruct, and certify. The documents multiply: site visit
reports, site instructions, snag lists, progress against phases, RFIs and consultant
responses, and the minutes of every site meeting.

The decisions here are protective. A site instruction issued in writing, minutes issued
after each meeting, an inspection recorded before concealment — these settle disputes about
who asked for what and whether it was done to specification. A [structured site-visit and
supervision record](/architecture-site-visit-software) turns a scattered stream of WhatsApp
photos into a defensible timeline. Interim certificates for the contractor's running bills
are also issued here, if that sits within your scope.

### 6. Completion and handover (COA Stage 7)

The building is finished; the paperwork is not. Handover produces the final documents:
as-built ("completion") drawings reflecting what was actually built, the completion /
occupancy certificate from the authority, warranties and O&M manuals collected from the
contractor and vendors, and a closed-out snag list. The decision this stage produces is the
formal one that the project is complete and accepted — which releases the final fee and,
just as importantly, marks the point from which any defects-liability period runs.

Close the project properly and it becomes a reference and a source of lessons. Close it
loosely and you are still fielding "can you send me that drawing" calls two years later.

## Where the fee and the invoice fit

COA's scale ties fee release to stage completion — a small share at concept, the largest
share across working drawings and tender, and the balance through construction supervision.
The exact percentages belong in your engagement letter, but the principle is fixed: you
bill *against a stage the client has approved*, not against effort. Structuring proposals
around [COA fee stages](/architect-fee-proposal-software) keeps your billing legible to the
client and keeps cash roughly in step with delivery.

When you raise those stage invoices, architectural services fall under **SAC 998321** and
attract **18% GST** — split as CGST + SGST within the state or IGST across states. A
stage-linked billing plan makes [GST-compliant invoicing](/gst-billing-software-architects-india)
straightforward: each stage sign-off is a clean trigger for a correctly numbered,
correctly taxed invoice. For the wider tax picture — reverse charge, TDS, input credit,
26AS/AIS/GSTR reconciliation — see our
[GST guide for architecture firms](/blog/gst-guide-for-architecture-firms-india).

## Keeping the whole lifecycle on one record

The stages are simple to describe and hard to run, because each hands documents and
decisions to the next, and the handoffs are where things fall through. The concept sign-off
that justifies a later revision, the sanctioned drawing a working drawing must match, the
site instruction that explains a cost — these only help you if they are findable.

That is the thinking behind keeping a project's phases, drawings, decisions, approvals and
site records on [one project record](/architecture-project-management-software) rather than
across email, drives and chat threads. AORMS is built around exactly this arc — proposal to
handover — so the gate you passed at concept still means something when you are certifying a
running bill eighteen months later. For the revision side, our post on [building a drawing
approval workflow](/blog/drawing-approval-workflow) goes a level deeper.

## Frequently asked questions

### What are the COA stages of architectural work?

The Council of Architecture's Comprehensive Architectural Services run, in sequence:
concept design; preliminary design and drawings; drawings for statutory approvals; working
drawings and tender documents; appointment of contractors; construction and periodic site
supervision; and final completion. Each stage has defined deliverables, and the COA scale
of charges ties the release of your fee to completing them.

### At which stage do I bill the client?

You bill against stages the client has approved, not against hours worked. A typical
engagement releases a small share of the fee at concept, larger shares through preliminary
and working-drawing stages, and the balance across construction supervision and completion.
Fix the exact percentages in your engagement letter, then raise a GST invoice (SAC 998321,
18%) at each agreed stage.

### When can construction legally start?

Only after the statutory authority sanctions the building and issues the building permit /
commencement certificate. Work begun without it is unauthorised and can attract penalties,
demolition orders, or refusal of the occupancy certificate later. Treat the sanction as a
hard gate between the approval stage and any site work.

### What is the difference between working drawings and GFC drawings?

They are usually the same set. "Working drawings" is the COA-stage term for the detailed,
dimensioned, coordinated set that makes the design buildable; "GFC" (good for construction)
is the site's name for the specific, approved, current version they are permitted to build
from. The point of both is that the site builds from one controlled version — never a
superseded sheet.

### Do small residential projects follow all these stages?

Yes, in compressed form. A small house still moves through brief, concept, sanction
drawings, working drawings and site supervision — the deliverables are lighter and some
stages overlap, but skipping the gates (an unrecorded concept sign-off, an unsanctioned
start) causes the same disputes on a bungalow as on a campus. The discipline scales down
better than the risk does.
