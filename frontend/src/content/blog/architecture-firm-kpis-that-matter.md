Most architecture practices watch the loud numbers — revenue this month, cash in the
bank, enquiries in the pipeline. Those matter, but they are lagging indicators: by the
time they move, the decision that moved them was made months ago, on a project you have
half-forgotten.

The KPIs that actually help a small or mid-size Indian practice are quieter. They tell
you whether the work you are doing right now is profitable, whether a project is drifting,
and whether your team is carrying a fair load — while there is still time to act. Here are
six, with why each matters and how to compute it. None needs expensive software, only the
discipline to record a few facts as work happens.

## 1. Fee realisation against COA stage

The Council of Architecture's Conditions of Engagement break a project fee into work
stages — concept design, preliminary design, statutory-approval drawings, working
drawings and tender documents, construction supervision, completion — each carrying a
percentage of the total fee. **Fee realisation** asks: for the stage you have actually
delivered, how much of the fee have you billed and collected?

Architects routinely run ahead of their billing. You finish working drawings — Stage 4
on most schedules — while invoicing is still stuck at the preliminary-design instalment:
60% of the value delivered, 30% of the fee billed. That gap is not profit you are waiting
on; it is profit you are quietly financing for the client, and it widens the longer a
project runs.

> Realisation = (fee billed to date ÷ fee earned by stage delivered) × 100

At 100% you are billed in step with delivery. Below 80% you are working for free in the
short term; well above 100% you have billed ahead of work, which invites a fee dispute
later. Structuring the proposal around COA stages in the first place makes the maths fall
out automatically — see [COA-compliant billing](/coa-compliant-billing-software) and
[fee-proposal structure](/architect-fee-proposal-software).

## 2. Revision ratio and revision source mix

The **revision ratio** is how many rounds of change a deliverable goes through before
sign-off. The **source mix** is the more important half: for each revision, why it
happened and who caused it — a client changing their mind, a genuine internal error, a
technical query, or a real change of scope.

A high count is not automatically bad. Three rounds because the client kept refining a
brief they are paying you to explore is healthy work; three rounds because the same
dimension was drawn wrong twice is a quality problem. The raw count cannot tell them
apart — only the source can. And the split has commercial meaning: client-driven and
scope-change revisions are usually billable, while internal errors are cost you absorb.

To compute it, tag each revision with a category (minor, major, critical) and a source
(client, internal error, technical query, scope change). A rising share of internal errors
points at drawing quality or coordination; a rising share of client and scope changes
points at a brief that was never pinned down — and at fees you should be recovering. More
in [architecture revision tracking](/architecture-revision-tracking) and
[the hidden cost of untracked revisions](/blog/hidden-cost-untracked-revisions).

## 3. Work-in-progress and unbilled fees

**Work-in-progress (WIP)** is the value of work done but not yet invoiced; **unbilled
fees** are the subset billable now but not yet raised. It is the closest thing a practice
has to hidden cash — and practices die of poor cash flow far more often than poor design.
WIP hides in a stage completed but not invoiced, a site instruction carried out but never
billed, an approval drawing submitted while the invoice sits in a draft folder.

To compute it, add the fee value of work delivered but not yet invoiced, per project.
Watch the total (how much cash is trapped) and the age; anything beyond 30–45 days
deserves an invoice this week. Remember your invoice total and your net receivable differ
once corporate clients deduct TDS on professional fees, so track the receivable, not the
headline. Clean [GST invoicing](/gst-billing-software-architects-india) tied to project
stages keeps WIP from accumulating in the first place.

## 4. Utilisation

Utilisation is the share of your team's paid time that goes to billable project work,
rather than admin, business development, training, and rework. Salaries are the largest
cost in almost every practice: if a designer is paid for 8 hours and 4 go to billable
work, your effective cost per billable hour has doubled, and no fee scale survives that.
This is not about squeezing people — it is about seeing where paid time goes, so you can
tell whether a thin month is a fee problem or a time-leak problem.

> Utilisation = (billable hours ÷ total available hours) × 100

You need time attributed to work, not just attendance. For a design practice a
sustainable band is usually 60–75%; persistently higher and people head for burnout with
no slack for the inevitable revision, persistently lower and either fees are underquoted
or non-billable work has crept in unmeasured. Attributing time as work happens — rather
than reconstructing timesheets on a Friday — is what makes the number trustworthy.

## 5. Approval turnaround

Approval turnaround is the time between issuing something for a decision — a drawing, a
client sign-off, a consultant response — and getting it back. Idle time between approvals
is invisible and expensive: a drawing waiting three weeks for a client decision is three
weeks of schedule gone and a site that may sit paused. Because nobody is working during
the wait, it never shows up in a task list, yet it is often the largest single chunk of a
project's elapsed time.

Record the date each item is issued and the date the decision returns; the difference is
turnaround. Average it by client and by approval type. Long turnarounds concentrated on
one client are often the evidence you need to justify an extension of time — and a
timestamped record of what was issued and when it was approved is your first defence in a
"we never approved that" dispute. See
[approval workflow](/architecture-approval-workflow-software).

## 6. A fair team score

A single productivity metric is easy to game and often rewards the wrong behaviour: the
person who quietly prevents revisions by getting a drawing right the first time looks
*less* productive on a task counter than the person generating rework. A balanced
composite is far harder to fool and fairer to careful work.

Weight several dimensions rather than one. The approach we use, ASPRF, is a rolling
30-day score weighted roughly: reliability 30%, quality 25%, client impact 15%,
collaboration 15%, learning 10%, wellbeing 5% (opt-in). The exact weights matter less than
the principle — measure a spread of what good work looks like, keep it rolling so a single
bad week does not define someone, and be transparent about the formula. More in
[measuring team performance](/blog/measuring-team-performance-asprf).

## Putting it together

You do not need all six from day one. Start with **fee realisation against stage** and
**revision source mix** — between them they explain most of where a practice's money leaks
— and let the rest follow as the recording habit takes hold. That habit is the real
prerequisite: every KPI here depends on capturing a fact at the moment it occurs, instead
of reconstructing it later from memory and email. A practice-management system like AORMS
makes that capture a by-product of doing the work, so the numbers are already there when
you need them — but they matter whether or not you use software to keep them. Measure the
quiet things, and the loud ones tend to look after themselves.

## Frequently asked questions

### Which KPIs should a small architecture practice start with?

Start with fee realisation against COA stage and revision source mix. The first tells you
whether you are billing in step with the work delivered; the second whether your revisions
are billable client changes or unbillable internal rework. Between them they explain most
profit leakage. Add WIP, utilisation, approval turnaround and a team score as the recording
habit settles in.

### How is fee realisation different from just tracking revenue?

Revenue tells you what you invoiced; realisation tells you whether that invoicing kept pace
with the work delivered against the COA stage schedule. A project can show healthy revenue
while you have quietly delivered a stage ahead of your billing — real profit you are
financing for the client without meaning to. Realisation surfaces that gap while you can
still raise the invoice.

### What is a good utilisation rate for an architecture firm?

For a design-led practice, a sustainable band is usually 60–75% of paid time on billable
work. Higher signals burnout risk and no slack for revisions; lower usually means fees are
underquoted or non-billable time has crept in unmeasured. Treat the band as a starting
point, not a target to maximise — the right number depends on your mix of design and site
work.

### Do I need software to track these KPIs?

No. Every KPI here can live in a spreadsheet if you have the discipline to record the
underlying facts as work happens. Software mainly makes that capture a by-product of the
work rather than a separate end-of-month chore, which is usually where manual tracking
breaks down.
