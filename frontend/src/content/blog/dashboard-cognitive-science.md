A principal architect does not sit and stare at a screen all day. They glance.
Between a site call, a client meeting, and a redline, they get maybe ten seconds
to ask one question: *is anything on fire, and if so, where?*

A dashboard built for that reality looks nothing like a trading terminal.

## The two dashboards we deliberately did not build

**The Bloomberg terminal** packs maximum data into every pixel. It works because
a trader stares at it continuously and is trained to parse density. An architect
is neither. Density here is not power — it is noise that hides the one thing that
matters.

**The military / NOC dashboard** is built around alerts: red lights, severity
levels, alarms. It assumes a dedicated operator whose job *is* watching. Give
that to a busy owner and you get **alarm fatigue** — when everything can shout,
nothing is heard, and the screen starts to feel like an accusation: *your office
is unhealthy.*

## What we built instead

AORMS treats **attention as the scarce resource**, not data. The dashboard's job
is to protect it.

- A simple shape language — **● running smoothly · ▲ attention soon · ■ act
  now** — in two colours, white and one blue.
- **Exactly one** blue marker on the whole screen: the single highest-leverage
  action. Never two competing emergencies.
- Most of the office is fine most of the time, so the screen *says so*. Calm is
  the default state, not an empty one.
- After you act, it tells you the **outcome** in plain words — billing recovers,
  delivery stays on schedule — not a confidence percentage.

The goal is not "here is all your office data." It is: *I reviewed the office.
Only this matters now. Everything else is handled.*

That is a cognitive stance, not a colour scheme.
