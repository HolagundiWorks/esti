Pricing is the part of practice most architects were never taught. You can design a
building, run a site and detail a staircase, and still feel uncertain quoting a fee for the
whole thing. The result is familiar: a number picked to win the job, a scope that was never
written down, and a client who assumes "the fee" covers every change they can think of. This
is a working guide to pricing architectural services in India — the Council of Architecture's
fee framework, the three common ways to structure a fee, invoicing against stages, and pricing
the revisions that quietly eat a practice's margin.

## The COA fee framework, and what it actually is

The **Council of Architecture (COA)** publishes *Conditions of Engagement and Scale of
Charges* — the reference most Indian practices reach for. Two things about it are worth being
clear-eyed on.

First, it is **guidance, not a legally enforceable price floor.** Competition law in India
takes a dim view of professional bodies mandating minimum fees, so the scale is best treated
as a widely-used reference point you can depart from by agreement — not a rate you are bound to
charge. Second, its real value is not the headline percentage; it is the **stage structure**.
The COA breaks comprehensive architectural services into defined work stages, each carrying a
share of the total fee — and that ties money to delivered work rather than to a single lump you
have to justify at the end.

A representative COA-style stage split for comprehensive services looks like this (the exact
percentages are set in your agreement — treat these as illustrative, not official):

| Stage | Work | Share of fee |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Concept design | ~10% |
| 2 | Preliminary design and drawings | ~20% |
| 3 | Drawings for statutory / client approval | ~15% |
| 4 | Working drawings and tender documents | ~25% |
| 5 | Tender action, appointment of contractor | ~5% |
| 6 | Construction — periodic site inspection | ~20% |
| 7 | Completion | ~5% |

The point is the shape, not the decimals: roughly two-thirds of the fee is earned before a
brick is laid, in the design and documentation stages where the intellectual work sits.

## Three ways to structure the fee

### Percentage of project cost

The COA basis. Your fee is a percentage of the **cost of works** — the estimated, and later
actual, construction cost, excluding land, your own fee, and statutory charges. For
comprehensive services the reference point is often cited around **5% and upward**, scaling
with the project's category and complexity (a hospital or a factory earns more than a simple
residence for the same built-up area).

It self-corrects: if the client upgrades the specification, the cost of works rises and so
does your fee. The friction is that the base is a moving number, so agree explicitly how it is
computed and when it is trued up against actual cost.

### Lump-sum (fixed) fee

A single agreed figure for a defined scope. Clients love the certainty, and it reads cleanly
in a proposal. It only works when the **scope is genuinely pinned down** — the moment the
brief moves and your fixed fee does not, you are absorbing the difference. Never quote a
lump sum without writing down exactly what it buys and what triggers an extra.

### Area-based (per square foot)

Common in Indian residential practice: a rate per square foot of built-up area. Simple to
quote and easy for a client to understand, but decoupled from actual cost and effort — the
same ₹/sq ft on a bare shell and on a heavily-serviced, detail-rich home rewards the first and
punishes the second. Useful as a quick benchmark; risky as your only basis on a complex
project.

Most practices mix these — a percentage or lump sum for the core design, hourly for open-ended
advisory, and a clear line that reimbursables sit outside the fee entirely.

## Stage-wise invoicing

Whatever basis you choose, **bill against stages, not milestones you invent later.** Take a
mobilisation advance on signing, then raise an invoice on completion of each stage — concept,
preliminary, approval drawings, working drawings, and periodic bills through construction. Two
disciplines keep this clean.

Watch **realisation** — the gap between the stage you have delivered and the fee you have
billed. Architects habitually run ahead, finishing working drawings while invoicing is still
stuck at the preliminary instalment. That gap is not profit you are waiting on; it is profit
you are financing for the client.

And get the **tax mechanics** right the first time. Architectural services are **SAC 998321–
998323, taxed at 18% GST** — CGST 9% + SGST 9% when the project site is in your state, IGST
18% when it is elsewhere. Corporate clients also deduct **income-tax TDS at 10% under Section
194J**, so your bank receipt is smaller than your invoice total; track the net receivable. The
deeper mechanics are in our [GST guide for architecture firms](/blog/gst-guide-for-architecture-firms-india)
and in [GST billing software for architects](/gst-billing-software-architects-india).

## What belongs in a fee proposal

A proposal that prevents disputes is specific about the boundary of the fee. Put in writing:

- **Scope of services** — which COA stages are included, and which are excluded (interiors,
  landscape, structural or MEP consultancy, third-party liaison).
- **Fee basis and amount** — percentage, lump sum or area rate, with the base defined.
- **Payment schedule** — the advance and stage instalments, each tied to a deliverable.
- **Reimbursables** — statutory fees, printing, travel, models, specialist consultants:
  normally billed at actuals *on top of* the fee, never inside it.
- **Revisions included** — how many rounds per stage the fee covers (see below).
- **Exclusions, validity and a GST note** — that the fee is exclusive of 18% GST.

Structuring the proposal around COA stages from the start makes the invoicing maths fall out
automatically. More in [COA-compliant billing](/coa-compliant-billing-software) and
[fee-proposal structure](/architect-fee-proposal-software).

## Pricing revisions and scope changes

This is where most fees leak. A fee covers a **reasonable number of iterations within a
stage** — you cannot quote a design fee that assumes the client accepts the first sketch.
State that number: for example, two rounds of revision per stage before sign-off. Once a
stage is approved and the client reopens it, that is an **additional service**, priced
separately and agreed *before* you do the work.

The discipline that makes this stick is recording **why** each change happened. A client
refining a brief they are paying you to explore is billable design work; redrawing your own
error is cost you absorb; a genuine change of scope is a fee revision. Tagging each revision
by source — client-driven, internal error, technical query, scope change — turns an awkward
"can we charge for this?" conversation into a documented fact. That is the argument in [the
hidden cost of untracked revisions](/blog/hidden-cost-untracked-revisions), and it is the
whole point of proper [change management](/architecture-change-management-software) and
[revision tracking](/architecture-revision-tracking).

## Frequently asked questions

### Is the COA fee scale mandatory in India?

No. The COA *Conditions of Engagement and Scale of Charges* is guidance, not a legally binding
minimum — competition law discourages professional bodies from mandating fees. Most practices
use it as a reference point and agree the actual fee and stage split with the client in the
engagement.

### How do architects usually charge — percentage or per square foot?

Both are common. Percentage-of-cost is the COA basis and scales with the project's ambition;
area-based (₹ per sq ft) is popular in residential work for its simplicity but is decoupled
from actual effort. Many firms mix them — a percentage or lump sum for core design, hourly for
open-ended advisory, and reimbursables billed separately at actuals.

### When should I invoice — upfront or on completion?

Neither extreme. Take a mobilisation advance on signing, then bill on completion of each COA
stage against a defined deliverable. Stage-wise invoicing keeps your billing in step with the
work delivered and stops you financing the project for the client.

### Should the fee include GST?

Quote the fee **exclusive of GST** and add 18% on the invoice — CGST 9% + SGST 9% if the site
is in your state, IGST 18% if elsewhere, under SAC 998321–998323. Say so plainly in the
proposal, so there is no dispute at billing time.

### How many revisions should a fee cover?

Enough for reasonable iteration within a stage — often two rounds per stage — stated explicitly
in the proposal. Once a stage is approved and reopened, treat further changes as an additional
service, priced and agreed before you start. Recording the source of each revision is what lets
you tell billable client changes from unbillable internal rework.
