There is a particular kind of tiredness that has nothing to do with hours worked. It is
the tiredness of opening a file you thought was finished, for the fourth time, to make a
change you are fairly sure the client asked you to make the *opposite* of three weeks ago.
You can't prove it. You make the change. And a little more of your goodwill, your fee, and
your enthusiasm for the project quietly drains away.

Most architects call this **revision frustration** and treat it as the weather — something
that happens to you, that you endure. It isn't. It is a record problem: no log of what was
requested, no category to show whether the change was minor or a re-plan, no link between
the request and the fee that should have moved with it. Record problems can be fixed —
and the fix is not a stricter client or a more disciplined team. It is a workflow where
every change has a source, a weight, and an approval before the drawings move.

## Why it feels inevitable

Design is iterative by nature. Clients *should* react to drawings; that reaction is the
work. The frustration doesn't come from the existence of revisions — it comes from three
things that almost always travel with them:

1. **No memory.** The change requested in a site meeting, confirmed on a phone call,
   contradicted in an email and re-confirmed over WhatsApp lives in five places and
   therefore nowhere. When the client later asks "who decided this?", you genuinely don't
   have a clean answer.
2. **No weight.** A one-line "can we just move the staircase" and a "let's reconsider the
   whole ground floor" are recorded — if at all — the same way: as a vague to-do. So you
   can't see that 70% of your effort is going into a handful of major, client-driven
   changes.
3. **No price.** Because the change was never captured as a change, it was never billable.
   It became "part of the service" by default, and the fee you agreed for three iterations
   silently now covers nine.

None of those three is a discipline failure. They are the predictable result of managing
a fluid, high-stakes conversation with tools — email, chat, a drawings folder — that were
never designed to hold a decision.

## The shift: treat a revision as data, not noise

The firms that escape revision frustration don't have more disciplined clients. They have
a different relationship with the change itself. Every revision carries two pieces of
information that change everything:

- **Where did it come from?** Client-driven, an internal error, a technical query, or a
  genuine scope change. This single field ends the "we never asked for that" argument,
  because the answer is recorded at the moment the change is made — not reconstructed
  defensively months later.
- **How big is it?** Minor, major, or critical. Recording the weight is what lets you see
  the shape of a project's rework instead of feeling buried by it.

Once those two facts live on every change, something quietly powerful happens: you can
look at a project and say, *"62% of the revisions on this job came from the client after
sign-off, and four of them were major."* That is no longer a feeling. It is a fact you can
take into a fee conversation.

## From resentment to a professional conversation

Here is the part that surprises people. Tracking revisions properly doesn't make you
adversarial with clients — it makes you *less* so. The resentment in the architect-client
relationship almost always comes from ambiguity: you feel taken advantage of, they feel
you're being precious about "small" changes, and neither of you has anything concrete to
point at.

When every change has a source, a weight, an approval and a link to the agreed fee, the
conversation stops being "did you ask for this?" and becomes "here's the record — these
three changes are within scope, and this fourth one is a scope change we'd raise as
additional fee." Clients respect that. It is what a professional sounds like. The drama
was never about the revisions; it was about the lack of a record.

## The takeaway

You will never have fewer revisions — and you shouldn't want to, because reacting to
drawings is how clients arrive at the right building. But you can stop *absorbing* them
blindly. The difference between a practice that dreads revisions and one that handles them
calmly is not willpower. It is whether the change is a recorded, weighted, billable fact —
or a vague memory you'll lose the argument about later.
