Most architects did not train to be tax accountants, yet GST touches almost every
invoice a practice raises. The rules are not especially hard, but they are specific,
and a handful of them are easy to get subtly wrong — the tax split, the place of
supply, which TDS applies, what you can claim back. Getting them right is not only a
compliance matter: an invoice raised with the wrong split or SAC code delays payment
and disrupts the fee recovery cycle a stage at a time. This is a working guide to the
GST an Indian architecture practice actually encounters. It is not a substitute for
your chartered accountant; it is meant to make that conversation shorter and your own
book-keeping cleaner.

## Which SAC code, and which rate

Services are classified under **SAC (Services Accounting Code)**, the services
counterpart of HSN. Architectural work sits in group **99832**:

- **998321** — architectural advisory services
- **998322** — architectural services for residential building projects
- **998323** — architectural services for non-residential building projects
- **998324** — historical restoration; **998325** — urban planning

Every one of these is taxed at **18% GST**, so the rate is rarely the hard part — the
code you print matters for correct classification, not for the amount of tax. Pick the
code that matches the work: advisory under 998321, project-based building design under
998322 or 998323. There is more on this in our note on the [HSN/SAC code for
architects](/hsn-sac-code-for-architects) and on [GST on architecture
services](/gst-on-architecture-services).

## Place of supply is your site, not your client's desk

This is the single most misunderstood rule for architects, and it decides whether you
charge CGST + SGST or IGST. The general GST rule points to the recipient's location — but
architectural services are treated as **directly related to immovable property**, and for
those, Section 12(3) of the IGST Act fixes the place of supply as **the location of the
property** — the site — not where the client's office is registered.

So the test is: *where is the building?*

- **Site in your own state** → the supply is intra-state → **CGST + SGST** (9% + 9%).
- **Site in another state** → inter-state → **IGST** (18% as a single line).

A Bengaluru firm designing a house in Bengaluru for a Mumbai-based client charges CGST +
SGST, because the property is in Karnataka — even though the client is in Maharashtra.
Following the client's state instead of the site's is the most common reason a GST
abstract refuses to reconcile at filing time. If the property is outside India, the place
of supply falls back to the recipient's location.

## Do you even need to register

Registration is mandatory once your **aggregate turnover crosses ₹20 lakh** in a
financial year (₹10 lakh in special-category states). A useful relief: unlike goods, a
service provider making **inter-state supplies is not forced to register** just because
the supply crossed a state line — the ₹20 lakh threshold still applies. Once registered,
you charge and file regardless of the client's size.

## Composition vs regular

A service provider with turnover up to **₹50 lakh** can opt for the **composition
scheme** under Section 10(2A): a flat **6%** (3% CGST + 3% SGST) on turnover, a **bill of
supply** instead of a tax invoice, and lighter filing. The catch is real, though — you
**cannot collect GST from the client, cannot claim input tax credit, and cannot make
inter-state supplies.**

For most practices with corporate clients, the **regular scheme** wins for one reason:
your client wants the **input tax credit** on your 18%, and under composition you pass
nothing on. Composition suits very small studios billing individual homeowners who cannot
use ITC anyway. Match the scheme to who your clients are, not to the headline percentage.

## Two kinds of TDS — don't confuse them

Architects routinely meet two completely different deductions both called "TDS":

- **Income-tax TDS under Section 194J.** Corporate and other non-individual clients
  deduct **10%** on professional fees (threshold ₹30,000 per financial year per nature of
  payment). This is *income tax* deducted against your PAN — you reclaim it in your ITR,
  and it shows up in **Form 26AS / AIS**. It does not reduce your GST liability.
- **GST TDS under Section 51.** Government departments, PSUs and notified bodies deduct
  **2%** GST (1% CGST + 1% SGST, or 2% IGST) on contracts above ₹2.5 lakh. This is *GST*,
  credited to your electronic cash ledger and visible in GSTR-7.

The practical consequence: your **invoice total and net receivable are different
numbers**. A ₹1,00,000 + 18% invoice to a company nets ₹1,08,000 in the bank (₹1,18,000
less ₹10,000 of 194J TDS), with the ₹10,000 recovered later against 26AS — so track the
receivable, not the headline total.

## What a compliant tax invoice must carry

A GST tax invoice for services must be issued **within 30 days of supply** and must
contain: your name, address and **GSTIN**; a **serial number unique and consecutive for
the financial year** (up to 16 characters); the date; the client's name, address and
GSTIN if registered; the **SAC code** and description; the taxable value; the **tax rate
and amount shown as CGST/SGST or IGST separately**; the **place of supply** for
inter-state invoices; whether any line is on **reverse charge**; and a signature. The
FY-sequential numbering matters more than firms expect — a gap or a mid-year reset is
exactly what a scrutiny picks up. There is more on this in [GST billing software for
architects](/gst-billing-software-architects-india) and the earlier post on [GST
invoicing done right](/blog/gst-invoicing-for-architects).

## Reverse charge you might actually owe

Reverse charge (RCM) flips who pays: the *recipient* remits the GST. You rarely charge on
reverse charge, but you may **owe** it as a buyer — on **legal services from an advocate**,
**goods transport (GTA)**, **import of services** (a foreign software or design tool), or a
**sitting fee to a director**. When RCM applies you pay the tax and, if eligible, claim it
back as ITC in the same return. Overlooking RCM on inbound services is a quiet source of
notices.

## Input tax credit: what you can and can't claim

Registered practices can claim **ITC** on genuine business inputs — software
subscriptions, office rent, professional fees, printing, hardware — provided four things
hold: a valid tax invoice, the service actually received, the supplier having paid and
filed (so it appears in your **GSTR-2B**), and a claim within the time limit. Credit not
reflected in 2B is not yours to take, however valid your own invoice looks.

Some credits are **blocked under Section 17(5)** regardless: motor vehicles, food and
beverages, club memberships, and **construction of immovable property on your own
account** — relevant if you are fitting out your own office.

## Reconciliation: 26AS, AIS and the GSTR trio

At year end, three matches keep you clean: **output tax** in your books against **GSTR-1**
(outward) and **GSTR-3B** (summary and payment); **ITC claimed** against **GSTR-2B**; and
**TDS in your books** against **Form 26AS** and the **AIS**, which now also surface your
reported GST turnover.

Reconciliation is tedious because these live in different portals with different cut-offs.
The discipline that saves you is upstream: raise every invoice with the correct split, code
and number the first time, so the abstract adds up before you open the portal.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the GST rate on architectural services in India?

18%. It is charged as CGST 9% + SGST 9% when the project site is in your own state, or as
IGST 18% when the site is in another state. All the SAC codes in group 99832 carry the same
18% rate.

### Which SAC code should an architect use?

Use the code that matches the work: 998321 for architectural advisory, 998322 for
residential building projects, 998323 for non-residential building projects. The code drives
correct classification, not the tax amount — every code in the group is taxed at 18%.

### Is the place of supply the client's location or the site?

The site. Architectural services are treated as directly related to immovable property, so
under Section 12(3) of the IGST Act the place of supply is where the building is located —
which is what determines whether you charge CGST + SGST or IGST, regardless of where the
client is registered.

### Why is my bank receipt less than my invoice total?

Because corporate clients deduct income-tax TDS at 10% under Section 194J on your
professional fees. That deduction is credited against your PAN and shows in Form 26AS; you
recover it in your income-tax return. Track your *net receivable*, not the headline total.
