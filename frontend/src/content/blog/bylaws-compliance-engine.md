The most expensive mistakes in a project are made on day one — when a concept is
developed against a feasibility the plot does not actually allow. AORMS moves
that check to the front.

## FAR done correctly

The headline rule is simple:

```text
site area × FAR = maximum permissible built-up
```

The detail is where firms slip. That maximum is a **gross** limit. The number you
must compare against it is the **net** proposed built-up — *after* excluding the
areas the bylaw does not count: parking, staircases, lifts, ramps, machine rooms,
open balconies, ducts, and water tanks.

The engine compares net proposed against the gross limit, so the answer reflects
what will actually be sanctioned — not an optimistic gross figure that collapses
at scrutiny.

## Encoded by jurisdiction

Bylaws are local. AORMS carries the rules per jurisdiction — BBMP and beyond —
covering ground coverage, setbacks, height, and the exclusion list, so a check in
Bengaluru is not a check written for somewhere else.

## A feasibility gate, not a formality

The compliance check is meant to run **early and often** — at site assessment,
before concept is committed, and whenever the proposal moves. There is even a
public compliance widget you can embed to let a prospect test a plot themselves.

Good design that cannot be sanctioned is not good design. The engine makes that
constraint visible while it can still change the outcome.
