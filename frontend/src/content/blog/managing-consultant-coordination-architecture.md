On any project larger than a modest residence, the architect is rarely working alone. A
structural engineer sizes the frame, an MEP consultant lays out electrical and HVAC, a PHE
consultant runs the water and drainage, and — depending on the building — a fire consultant,
a façade specialist, a landscape architect and a vertical-transport vendor all add their
drawings to the pile. As the lead consultant, the architect's job is not only to design the
building but to make all of these disciplines agree with each other, and with the same
version of the plan, on the same dates.

That work is invisible when it goes well and catastrophic when it fails. A beam that lands
across a doorway, a duct that will not fit above a false ceiling, a shaft that three
consultants have each sized differently — these are not design failures but *coordination*
failures, and almost all of them trace to the same root cause: the disciplines were working
from different versions of the truth, and nobody had a record of who was told what, when.

This is a plain guide to running that coordination well — the mechanics of RFIs, drawing
transmittals and version control — and to the failure modes that a shared record quietly
removes.

## The core problem: everyone drifts to their own version

Coordination breaks not through a single dramatic error but through slow divergence. The
architect shifts a column grid to make a room work. The change is real and correct — but the
structural engineer is still detailing against last month's grid, the MEP consultant has
routed a cable tray along a wall that has since moved, and the PHE drawings show a shaft in
its old position. Each discipline is internally consistent and mutually wrong.

The reason is that a design set is not one document; it is many drawings held by many people,
updated on their own schedules. Unless there is a deliberate mechanism to keep them aligned,
entropy wins — and the gaps only surface when the drawings are overlaid, often at site, the
most expensive possible place to find them.

Good coordination is the set of habits that keep that drift from accumulating. There are
three: a disciplined **RFI trail**, controlled **transmittals**, and strict **version
control** over one coordinated set.

## RFIs: turn questions into records

An RFI — a request for information — is simply a formal question from one party to another.
The structural engineer asks whether a slab can be dropped; the contractor asks which of two
conflicting dimensions to build to; the MEP consultant asks where the architect wants the AHU
room. In practice, most of these questions are asked over a phone call or a WhatsApp group,
answered verbally, and then lost.

The loss is the whole problem. A verbal answer settles the question only for as long as
everyone remembers it. Two months later, when the site has built to the answer and the client
disputes it, there is no record of who asked, what was answered, or on what date — and the
question gets re-litigated from memory, a poor witness on a project with forty open issues.

A disciplined RFI trail fixes this by making every consequential question a small, numbered
record with four fields: **who asked**, **what they asked**, **who answered**, and **when**.
That is all it takes; the RFI needs no heavy process, only to exist as a lookup instead of a
memory. When a question is answered on the record, the answer is durable — it survives staff
turnover, settles disputes without argument, and tells you which open questions are still
blocking work. A structured [RFI trail across your
consultants](/rfi-management-software-for-architects) turns coordination from a series of
disappearing conversations into a standing register you can actually manage.

The same principle applies to the questions a client raises in a meeting. Recording and
issuing [minutes of meeting](/minutes-of-meeting-software-for-architects) on the project
record — rather than in a personal notebook — gives client instructions the same durability
an RFI gives consultant queries.

### Chasing is a job, not a hope

An RFI that is raised but never answered is worse than one never raised, because work is now
blocked on it. The most common coordination failure is not a wrong answer — it is an RFI that
sits open for three weeks because chasing it was nobody's explicit job. Assign that job:
someone should be able to see, in one view, every open RFI and its age, and follow up on the
stale ones before they stall a whole discipline.

## Transmittals: prove what was issued

When you send a revised drawing to a consultant, that act needs a record — a **transmittal**
listing what was sent, to whom, and on what date. Without it, a tedious argument becomes
unwinnable: the consultant swears they were never sent the latest floor plate, you are certain
you emailed it, and neither of you can prove anything.

A transmittal is the proof. It converts "I think I sent it" into "here is the dated record
that R3 went to the structural engineer on 12 June" — collapsing a class of disputes that
otherwise eat hours. It also protects against the most expensive site error of all — a
contractor or consultant building from a **superseded** drawing — because the trail shows
exactly which version each party currently holds. Treating each issue as a logged [document
approval and transmittal event](/architect-document-approval-system) rather than an email
attachment is what makes a coordinated set defensible.

## Version control: one coordinated set, not many

The purpose of RFIs and transmittals is to protect a single thing: **one coordinated set**
that every discipline is genuinely working against. Version control is the discipline that
keeps that set honest.

The rules are simple to state and hard to keep without a system:

- Every drawing has a **current** version and a history of **superseded** ones.
- When a drawing is revised, the old version is marked superseded, not deleted — you need the
  history to answer "what did we build to?"
- The change is **transmitted** to every affected discipline, with a record.
- Nobody works from a drawing they cannot confirm is current.

When those rules hold, a moved column grid propagates: the architect issues the revised plan,
the transmittal reaches the structural and MEP consultants, and each updates against a version
they know is current. When they fail, the grid moves in one set and not the others, and the
clash waits silently for the site to find it. A tight [drawing revision and version-control
workflow](/drawing-revision-tracking-software) — current versus superseded, who was issued
what, when — is what keeps the coordinated set coordinated; our post on [building a drawing
approval workflow](/blog/drawing-approval-workflow) goes deeper into the states each drawing
should move through.

## The failure modes, and what actually fixes them

Nearly every coordination failure is a version of one of these:

- **Disciplines drift to different versions.** Fixed by version control plus transmittals —
  one current set, changes issued with a record.
- **Answers are verbal and vanish.** Fixed by an RFI trail — every consequential question
  answered on the record.
- **RFIs sit open and block work.** Fixed by giving chasing an explicit owner and a view of
  RFI age.
- **"We never received that revision."** Fixed by the transmittal record — dated proof of
  what went to whom.
- **Coordination lives across email, drives and three WhatsApp groups.** Fixed by keeping the
  whole thing on one project record, so any party's current version and any question's answer
  is a lookup, not an excavation.

The common thread is that coordination fails in the *gaps between people*, and every fix is
some form of writing the handoff down. That is exactly what a shared record does. AORMS keeps
a project's drawings, revisions, transmittals, RFIs, consultant responses and client minutes
on [one project record](/architecture-project-management-software), so the architect, as lead
consultant, can see what is current, what is open and who is waiting — without reconstructing
it from memory. For the wider picture, see [architecture consultancy
management](/architecture-consultancy-management-software).

## Frequently asked questions

### Who is responsible for consultant coordination on a project?

The architect, as the lead or principal consultant, ordinarily carries coordination
responsibility — reconciling the structural, MEP, PHE and specialist drawings into one
buildable set and resolving clashes between them. The individual consultants are responsible
for the correctness of their own designs, but the architect owns the integration. That is why
the RFI trail and the transmittal record matter: they are the evidence of how coordination was
actually conducted.

### What is the difference between an RFI and a drawing transmittal?

An RFI is a *question* — a request for information from one party to another, answered on the
record. A transmittal is the *issue* of a document — a dated record of which drawings were
sent, to whom. RFIs manage the flow of decisions; transmittals manage the flow of drawings.
A coordinated project needs both: the RFI settles what was decided, the transmittal proves
what version everyone was given.

### How do you keep structural and MEP drawings coordinated with the architecture?

Maintain one coordinated set under strict version control: every drawing has a single current
version, revisions supersede rather than overwrite, and each change is transmitted to every
affected discipline with a record. The mechanism that keeps drift from accumulating is the
transmittal — when the architect moves a grid, the change only stays coordinated if it
demonstrably reaches the structural and MEP consultants and they update against it.

### Why not just use a WhatsApp group for coordination?

A group chat is fine for quick prompts but fails as a coordination record. Questions and
answers scroll away, there is no reliable version history, no way to tell which RFIs are still
open, and no dated proof of who was issued which drawing. When a dispute arises months later,
a chat log is nearly impossible to reconstruct into a defensible timeline. The chat can
complement a project record; it cannot replace one.

### What happens when a consultant works from a superseded drawing?

Whatever they produce against it is, by definition, coordinated to the wrong version — a
detail sized for a column that has moved, a duct routed through a wall that no longer exists.
The error usually surfaces at site, where it is most expensive to fix. A transmittal trail
prevents it by making each party's current version explicit, so "which revision are you
working to?" is answerable before anything is built.
